The morning circuit had always been Dango's way of being in the world — a full loop of the garden before breakfast, checking everything, knowing everything, the particular satisfaction of a territory understood. He had been doing it since he was old enough to know the garden well, and it had become part of him in the way that long habits become part of their practitioners: not just a thing he did but a thing he was.

He was good at motion. He was good at coverage, at presence, at the fast accumulation of facts about his environment. What he was less practiced at — what he had perhaps never had occasion to practise — was the other kind of knowing: the kind that requires you to stop.

The root near the old fence ended the question of practice. It had been there all summer, as roots are, doing nothing in particular. On a Tuesday morning in early June, Dango's right front paw found it at full speed. He stumbled, stopped, lifted the paw. It hurt clearly, the way things hurt when something has actually happened to them rather than just threatening to.

Gabu came over, inspected the paw with his characteristic unhurried attention, and delivered the verdict: "Rest it. Two or three days. No running."

Dango looked at the garden — the whole length of it, the corners unvisited, the circuit incomplete — with the expression of someone confronting an obligation they are not sure their character is built to meet.

"Two or three days," he said."Yes," said Gabu, already turning away. He did not elaborate. He had no interest in softening the prescription or negotiating its terms.

Dango sat down by the blue stone. He would learn to be still. He was not sure he knew how, but he would learn.


## The First Day


The first hour established the problem clearly. Being in one place while the garden continued without him produced a specific variety of discomfort — not pain, not quite anxiety, but something in between, a low-grade urgency with nowhere to go. His eyes kept moving to the periphery: the corner by the far fence, the shaded area under the old tree, the places where things might be happening in his absence that would go unwitnessed and therefore, in some sense, unreal.

This was, he gradually understood, his mind's theory of itself: that it was the thing that made the garden's events count. That his knowledge of them was what made them matter.

The garden appeared to be managing without him anyway.

The small black beetle that lived somewhere near the porch — he had seen it hundreds of times in passing, a peripheral fact, categorised and dismissed — crossed the ground in front of him, approached the blue stone, went underneath it, came out the other side, turned around, and went underneath again. Dango watched it do this four times. The whole operation was purposeful and complete and, he realised, had been happening every day, with or without an audience, for as long as the beetle had existed.

It always does this, he thought. Every single day. I never knew because I was always going somewhere else.

Daru came in the afternoon and told stories — years of the garden's history that Dango had heard in fragments before but never followed all the way through, because following things all the way through requires the willingness to stay in one place. He listened. The stories were good. They were much better, he noticed, when you didn't keep leaving them.

Gabu sat on his other side in companionable quiet, reading the afternoon in his particular way. It occurred to Dango that Gabu had been doing this all along — sitting with things fully, giving his complete attention to the small facts of the present — and that what Gabu had always seemed to be doing, which was nothing, was in fact something very specific and very difficult.


## The Second Day


Suke-san arrived at the stone before Daru, before Gabu, in the cool and pale first hour of the morning. He sat down, adjusted his posture, and observed the garden's awakening with the composed attention that was, Dango had come to understand, his most characteristic mode of being present. He didn't say why he had come early. He simply had.

At midday, Dango asked the question he had been building since the day before: "Gabu — when you sit still, what are you doing? I always thought you were waiting. But you don't seem to be waiting for anything."

"Attending," Gabu said."Attending.""There's a difference between attending and waiting. Waiting is directed — you're holding yourself ready for something specific that hasn't arrived yet. Attending is different. You're not directed toward anything in particular. You're simply present — fully present, without reservation, to whatever is actually here." He paused. "Boredom is what happens when you're in a place but your mind is somewhere else, anticipating, regretting, wishing for something different. Attending is when you're entirely where you are. They feel nothing like each other.""Is attending difficult?""It takes practice. But the difficulty isn't what you'd expect. It's not effortful so much as it is a form of permission you give yourself."

"Permission to do what?"

"To let things be enough."

Dango sat with that. "Try closing your eyes," Gabu said. "Let whatever is here come to you, without looking for it."

He did.

A thrush in the high branches of the old tree — he had heard this bird every morning of his life, but never like this, never as this: the particular rise at the end of its call, which was, he noticed, shaped almost like a question.The wind moving through the tall dry grass near the fence — its own rhythm, independent of everything else, rustling and pausing and rustling again according to rules he had never bothered to notice.Far away, the low harmonic of bees working the late summer flowers — a sound he associated with warmth and abundance, which was its own kind of information about what the season was doing.

"I've walked through all of that every morning," he said, opening his eyes."Yes," Gabu said. "You walked through it. You didn't stay in it."

Suke-san, who had been listening without appearing to listen, said quietly: "Motion is a kind of noise.""Yes," said Gabu. "Exactly."


## The Third Day


Suke-san arrived before sunrise. The garden was still damp with dew, the light barely started, the birds only beginning. He was sitting at the stone when Dango came out, his ears forward, his expression composed in the way of someone who has decided something and is seeing it through.

"You're very early," Dango said.Suke-san looked at the garden for a moment before answering. "I've been thinking about the way you are with people who need something. You don't make it a thing. You don't arrive with an explanation or a commentary about why you're there. You simply appear, as if presence were the obvious response, which for you I think it genuinely is." He paused. "I've been trying to understand what that is — whether it's instinct or choice or a kind of knowledge — and I decided the only useful approach was to practise it. So I came early.""Is it working?""I'm not sure. But I notice that being here feels correct, and that the wondering about whether I'm doing it right is getting quieter."

Dango sat down next to him. Neither spoke for a while. The sun came up over the far fence and the dew on the grass began to turn to light.

"Thank you," Dango said eventually, meaning several things at once.

Suke-san gave one small nod, meaning that he had understood all of them.

That afternoon, the paw was fine. Dango walked, then walked faster, then stood looking at Gabu with the question in his expression.

"Tomorrow," Gabu said."Yes," said Dango. "Tomorrow."

He sat back down by the stone. The beetle made its round. The shadows moved. The afternoon attended to itself, and he let it.


## After


He ran the next morning. The paw held perfectly, and the sensation was as good as he had expected: the familiar ground, the speed, the whole garden opening up around him — kinetic and known, delivering its information in the usual fast way, the way motion made available. He ran the full loop and then the loop again, because he could, because his body was glad to.

He was mid-circuit, somewhere in the open middle of the garden, when he stopped.

Not for his paw. Not from tiredness. He stopped because he passed a particular point in the grass and something in him — some new instinct, recently acquired — said: here. Right here.

He sat down.

He let things come to him.The thrush at the top of the tree, the question at the end of its call. The bees in the far flower beds, the steady hum of their particular industry. The wind in the tall grass near the fence, which had its own rhythm and had always had its own rhythm and would always have it, whether or not anyone was there to hear.The garden, full of itself, full of its own events, entirely indifferent to whether it was being watched — which was, he realised, also what made it generous.

He sat for a while. Then he stood up and ran the rest of the circuit, fast and happy, all the way home.
