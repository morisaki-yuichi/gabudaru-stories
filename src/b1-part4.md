The garden had a new neighbour, and he had arrived with his position already decided: he would observe, he would be polite if spoken to, and he would not become involved.

He was a Shiba Inu puppy — soft red-brown fur, white chest, a curly tail that he held as high as dignity permitted. His ears were slightly too large for his current head and his paws were slightly too large for his current body, both being features he had decided to simply not acknowledge. His name was Suke-san, and he carried it with appropriate gravity.

On his first morning, he walked the top of the garden wall with his chin raised and his gaze professionally distant. He assessed the flowers, the trees, the arrangement of the clouds. He was not looking for company.

I am perfectly fine on my own, he thought, with considerable conviction. I have always been fine on my own. This is my natural state.

A butterfly drifted past, close enough that he could feel the air from its wings.

His tail moved. Just once.

He stopped it immediately and looked around.No witnesses. Good.


## The First Morning


Dango had been watching from the far end of the garden, and now he came running with the particular velocity that was his standard greeting for anything new.

Suke-san saw him coming and arranged himself into a posture of calm authority.

"Good morning," said Suke-san, with a small, precise bow. "I am Suke-san."

"I'm Dango! Come and meet my friends, they're right over here, you're going to love them, come on!"

Suke-san looked at him steadily. Then he looked at the garden. Then he followed, on the grounds that declining would have required more words than he felt like using.

Near the old porch, two tortoises were sitting in the morning light. A small blue stone sat at the entrance to their home. One was examining a leaf with scholarly intensity. The other was trying, with apparent determination, to get his neck to a flower that was clearly too high.

"Gabu! Daru! This is Suke-san! He just moved in!"

The one with the leaf looked up slowly. He had the kind of calm in his eyes that takes years to acquire. "Hello," he said. "I'm Gabu. You're welcome in this garden."

The other one spun round at remarkable speed. "Hi! I'm Daru! Have you explored the whole garden yet? There's a fat bird in the big tree — have you seen him? He's round like a ball with wings, it's incredible."

Suke-san studied them. They were slow, round, and apparently contented. "Hello," he said. "You are tortoises."

"Yes!" said Daru."You move very slowly," Suke-san observed."Yes," said Gabu. "It suits us."

There was a pause. Then Daru said: "Your ears are very impressive."

Suke-san drew himself up slightly. "They are entirely normal ears."

"They're wonderful," said Daru, with complete sincerity.

Suke-san was momentarily without a response. He covered this by looking at an interesting spot on the ground.

What kept him there a moment longer than he had planned was Gabu. Gabu had returned to his leaf without ceremony, without any apparent interest in whether Suke-san was impressed or not. He was simply there, doing his own thing, at his own pace, entirely self-contained.

Suke-san recognised that quality. He respected it.

"It was good to meet you," he said finally. He turned and walked back to the wall. He did not look back.

But he thought about the blue stone for longer than he expected to.


## The Rainy Afternoon


For three days, Suke-san maintained his routine of solitary wall-walking and careful non-involvement. He was managing it rather well, he thought.

On the fourth day, the sky made other plans.

He was on the wall when the first drops fell — cold, large, and immediately followed by more. He jumped down and tried two different trees in succession. Both leaked. He was standing in the middle of the garden, genuinely wet and rapidly becoming more so, when the thunder arrived.

A long, low BOOM that seemed to pass through the ground as much as the air.

Suke-san's paws locked. His ears went flat. He scanned the garden in all directions with impressive speed.He was fine. This was completely fine. He was merely reassessing his options.

Another BOOM.

He ran. Not in any direction he had chosen — his paws simply selected a route and he followed them, past the flowers, past the oak, and then stopped because he had arrived somewhere familiar without meaning to.

The porch. The blue stone at the door, catching the grey light.

"Suke-san!" Daru's head appeared. "Come in — there's room, and it's dry!"

Suke-san assessed the entrance. He assessed the rain. He walked in and sat near the door, precisely and with dignity.

"Were you scared?" Daru asked, with no particular judgment in the question."No," said Suke-san.

"That's interesting," Daru said thoughtfully. "My legs shake when the thunder's very loud. Even when I know it's just weather. I find that rather embarrassing, honestly."

Suke-san looked at him. "You admit that?"

"It's true, so why wouldn't I? Gabu says honesty is more restful than the alternative."

Outside, Dango was running wild circles in the rain, barking at intervals, apparently in a state of complete joy.

"He isn't troubled by storms at all?" Suke-san asked."Dango has exactly one fear in the world," Daru said."What is it?""The vacuum cleaner."

Suke-san watched Dango launch himself into a puddle with both front paws simultaneously. Something shifted in his chest — a small internal movement he couldn't entirely account for.

A laugh. Very nearly.He swallowed it back down.

Gabu, who had been sitting quietly watching the rain, said: "Would you like to know about the stone?"

"Yes," said Suke-san. He hadn't planned to say that. But there it was.

Daru told the whole story. Finding the stone, fixing it to the shell, losing it in Dango's digging, the search in the fading light, Dango's nose finding it in the dark earth. And Gabu's decision.

"He said it belonged at the door," Daru finished. "So all of us could see it every day. Not just him."

Suke-san was quiet for a moment. "You've built something together," he said. "Without meaning to."

"Most good things happen that way," Gabu said, from his corner.

The rain continued for a long time. Daru talked. Gabu listened and occasionally contributed. Suke-san sat near the door and didn't leave. At some point he stopped actively resisting and simply let the afternoon be what it was.

When the rain ended, he stood, shook his fur dry, and said: "Thank you. For the shelter."

"Come back whenever you like," Daru said. "We're usually here."

Suke-san looked at the stone one more time. Then he walked out into the washed, quiet garden.

His tail moved once, behind him.He didn't try to stop it this time.


## The Morning He Came Back


He told himself, on the way home, that he had simply been sheltering from the rain. That was all. He had used a convenient dry space and he was grateful for it, as he would have been grateful for any shelter. This did not constitute involvement.

He was back the next morning before the garden had fully woken up, sitting quietly near the porch because — well. He had been walking nearby.

"Good morning," Daru said, appearing from inside."Good morning," Suke-san said. "I was in the area."

Daru sat beside him without comment, and they watched the light move across the grass together in a silence that was, Suke-san noticed, entirely comfortable.

"It's different every morning," Daru said eventually. "Even when nothing happens.""Yes," Suke-san said. "I've noticed that."

Gabu arrived at his own pace and gave Suke-san his customary nod — steady, unhurried, completely without expectation. Then he went to his favourite spot in the sun.

Then Dango arrived, saw Suke-san, and became entirely overcome.

"YOU CAME BACK! I KNEW IT! I TOLD GABU YOU WOULD COME BACK!"

Suke-san sat up straight. "I was just—"

But Dango's ears were bouncing, and Dango was bouncing, and before Suke-san had finished deciding how he felt about all of this—

— his tail was wagging.He caught it after a moment. But a moment was enough.

"Your tail," said Daru, smiling."There was a wind," said Suke-san."There isn't any wind today," said Daru.

Suke-san looked at the sky for a while. He looked at the flowers. He looked at the very interesting ground.

"Perhaps a very small one," he said.

He sat down beside Daru. Dango settled in front of him, eyes bright, tail quietly going. Gabu did not move, but the lines around his old eyes were warm.

"Can you stay?" Daru asked."For a little while," Suke-san said.

The morning spread across the garden at its usual unhurried pace. Suke-san sat very still and tried not to look like he was enjoying himself.

He was not succeeding.
