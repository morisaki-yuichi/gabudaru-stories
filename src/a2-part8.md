Gabu had mentioned the market fountain exactly once, in passing, and somehow the outing had been arranged from that single mention. "I was there once, a long time ago," he had said. "There was a fountain in the square. I remember it being beautiful." And that was enough — Daru had immediately begun talking about going, and Dango had immediately agreed, and even Suke-san had said it sounded reasonable.

The town was, in many ways, the opposite of the garden. Dense where the garden was open. Loud where the garden was quiet. Moving in all directions at once where the garden moved slowly and with purpose.

Dango's head turned constantly, trying to follow everything. Suke-san walked with his usual careful composure, but his eyes were busy. They went ahead of Gabu and Daru, then came back, then went ahead again — the natural result of speed differences that the garden had never made obvious.

It was a good morning, right up until the truck.

It came down the narrow market street without much warning: a large delivery truck, horn blaring, and the crowd on the pavement surging back to make room. When it had passed and the crowd resettled, Dango and Suke-san looked around and found that Gabu and Daru were simply not there anymore.


## Two Paths


Suke-san's instinct was immediate: systematic retracing. They had come from the left, so they went left. The street looked familiar in a general way, which wasn't the same as knowing where they were. After a while, things stopped looking familiar even in a general way.

"Maybe right instead of left," Suke-san said. He said it in a measured tone that was doing a lot of work keeping something less measured underneath it.

They tried right. Wrong again.

Dango sat down on the pavement and looked up. On a window ledge one floor above them, a large grey cat was observing the street with the particular authority of someone who had done so for years.

"Excuse me," Dango called. "Do you know the market fountain?""The big stone one in the main square? Two streets south, then left," said the cat. She said it without moving, which impressed Dango considerably."Thank you very much."

Suke-san looked at the cat. Then at Dango. "A cat," he said.

"She's lived here a long time," Dango said. "She knows the streets.""You don't know how long she's lived here.""She looked like a long time," Dango said.

Suke-san considered this. It was not a rigorous argument. He found he couldn't make a rigorous counter-argument either, which was interesting. "Fine," he said. "Which way is south?"

Dango looked at the sun. "That way," he said, pointing.

Two streets in that direction, then left: a large open square with a stone fountain at its centre, the water catching the afternoon light.

Suke-san stood and looked at the fountain for a moment."The cat was right," he said."Yes," said Dango. Then: "You knew which way south was. I didn't."Suke-san looked at him. "You asked the cat. I wouldn't have."They both thought about this for a moment.


## The Waiting


When the crowd had cleared, Gabu had simply stopped. Not in confusion, but deliberately — he had found a spot out of the foot traffic and stood there, thinking, while Daru watched the people pass and tried to locate either Dango or Suke-san in the flow.

"Should we go and look?" Daru asked. He was trying to keep his voice steady and mostly succeeding."They'll find their way," Gabu said. "Let's go to the fountain. Suke-san will choose it as a meeting point.""How do you know he'll think of a meeting point?""Because that's how Suke-san thinks. He plans. He'll recognise that they're separated and immediately start working out a logical place to converge.""And the fountain is the most logical?""The most visible, the most central, the most memorable. Yes."

They made their way through the market — slowly, as they always did, but with direction now. Daru kept looking around. Gabu kept walking.

"Are you worried?" Daru asked finally."Yes," Gabu said. "A little.""You don't seem it.""Worry and movement are different things," Gabu said. "Being worried doesn't require stopping."

Daru was quiet for a moment. Then: "Have you been lost before? In a place like this?"

"Yes. Once, a long time ago. Much more lost than this, in a much bigger place.""What did you do?""I stopped. I thought about what I knew rather than what I didn't. Then I moved toward something I was sure of, rather than away from something I was afraid of.""Were you frightened?""Quite frightened," Gabu said simply. "I've learned that being frightened doesn't prevent clear thinking. It just makes it harder. You have to practise."

Daru thought about this for the rest of the walk. It rearranged something in how he understood Gabu — not the calm on the surface, but what the calm was sitting on top of.


## Finding Each Other


Dango saw them first: two small shapes emerging from the far side of the square, moving at their characteristic steady pace. He ran before he had decided to run, crossing the square at full speed, and arrived in a kind of controlled collision with Daru that was mostly affection and partly relief.

"I knew you'd come here," Daru said, holding on."Suke-san thought of it," Dango said.

Suke-san stood in front of Gabu. He had clearly thought about what to say, and he said it directly, without softening it: "I couldn't navigate. I tried to retrace our steps methodically and it didn't work. Dango found help. We made it here together, but I didn't lead us here."

Gabu looked at him. "You're here," he said. "And you know what worked and what didn't. That's more than most people come away with from being lost."

"The cat knew the streets," Dango added. "Suke-san knew which way south was. Between us we had enough.""A reasonable division of ignorance," Gabu said, with something in his expression that was not quite a smile but was close.

Gabu looked at the fountain. The water moved over the stone lip and fell in a thin curtain into the basin below. It was the same fountain. Much was the same as he remembered it.

"Is it how you remembered?" Daru asked."Mostly," Gabu said. "Which is usually the best you can hope for."
