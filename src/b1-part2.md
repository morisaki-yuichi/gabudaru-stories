Since the tomato adventure, Gabu and Daru had settled into a comfortable rhythm as a team. They complemented each other well, though not without friction: Gabu's caution occasionally struck Daru as excessive, while Daru's impulses occasionally alarmed Gabu. On the whole, however, they had struck a workable balance — one that was about to be tested.

For some weeks, Daru had been spending time near the tall wooden fence at the back of the garden, watching the shadows move through its gaps and wondering about the world on the other side.


## The Strange Noise


One afternoon, Gabu was asleep under a large leaf when Daru heard a faint sound coming from the direction of the fence. Scritch, scratch, scritch. It was the kind of sound that demanded investigation, and Daru was exactly the kind of tortoise who would comply with that demand.

He found a gap at the bottom of the fence — barely wide enough for a young tortoise to pass through, but wide enough. Pressing his face against it, he saw what appeared to be a completely different world: tall purple flowers, long grass that moved in small currents of air, and a dense bush laden with yellow berries. The whole thing looked as though it had been put there specifically for him to find.

"Gabu," he called back. "You are going to want to see this. I'm serious."

Gabu, who had woken at the first sound of Daru's voice, made his way over. He looked through the gap for a considerable moment. Then he looked up at the sky, which had acquired a particular quality of grey that he had seen before.

"I notice you're already planning to go through," he said. It wasn't a question.

"Just a quick look. Half an hour at most. We'd be back long before anything happens."

"Those clouds," Gabu said, "have the look of something that intends to happen quite quickly. If we were to get caught on the other side—"

"We won't be. I promise."

Gabu looked at the sky again. He looked at his friend's expression. He made the decision he would later describe as the worst and most predictable mistake of his life. "All right. But we leave the moment I say it's time."


## Into the Wild


The world on the other side of the fence was extraordinary in the way that foreign places always are: immediately, disorienting, and rather more dangerous-feeling than it had looked through a gap. The grass reached above their shells. Navigation was a matter of guesswork.

Daru found the yellow berry bush and sampled one cautiously. His expression changed.

"Gabu. Try one of these. Just one."

Gabu did. It tasted of something warm and floral, like a memory of a better afternoon. He would have liked another, but he was watching the sky.

The clouds had been moving while they were occupied with the berries. The wind had strengthened. The quality of light had shifted from gold to something the colour of old pewter.

"We need to leave now," Gabu said. "I'm not asking — we need to go."

He didn't finish the sentence. A deep BOOM of thunder arrived without warning and shook the earth beneath their feet. Three seconds later, the rain hit — not gradually, but all at once, as if a decision had been made.


## Lost in the Rain


They turned immediately and tried to locate the fence. But the rain had already flattened the grass in new directions, and everything looked different. The landmarks they had noted on the way in had been reorganised by the weather.

"Which direction is it?" Daru asked. His voice was controlled, but only barely. "Gabu, I can't work out which direction."

"Neither can I. Not yet. Stop moving — movement is making it worse."

The rain intensified. Water began running along the ground in fast rivulets that pushed against their legs. Daru, lighter than Gabu, felt his footing give way first. He slid towards a wide, fast-filling puddle before he could correct himself.

"Gabu—!"

Gabu had already spotted their exit. It wasn't the fence, but it would do: a large tree root arching out of the ground nearby, creating a small dry hollow underneath. "Run to that root! Now — under it, quickly!"

Daru scrambled to his feet and ran. He reached the root, ducked underneath, and immediately turned to help. Gabu arrived two seconds later, slipping on the last stretch of wet mud — and Daru grabbed hold and pulled until they were both under cover.


## Waiting it Out


The hollow was cramped and cold but dry. Outside, the storm appeared to be settling in for the duration.

For a while, neither of them spoke. Then Daru said: "I'm sorry. I told you we'd be fine, and I was wrong."

"You weren't entirely wrong," Gabu said, after a moment. "The berries were remarkable. The garden was beautiful. The timing was poor."

"You're being generous."

"I'm being accurate. Your instinct to explore isn't the problem, Daru. It's one of the things I value about you. The difficulty is that you sometimes forget that conditions matter. We should have checked the sky before we went through — both of us. That's not only on you."

Daru was quiet for a moment. "Both of us," he repeated. "Even though you told me not to go?"

"I followed you," Gabu said simply. "I could have insisted harder. I chose not to."

They sat together as the storm wore on, talking about food, about warmth, about what they would do differently and what they would keep exactly the same.


## The Way Home


The storm broke eventually, as storms do, and left behind it a garden rinsed clean and unnaturally bright. The flattened grass gave them clear sightlines, and they found the fence without difficulty. They stepped back through the gap carefully, shook the water from their shells, and stood on familiar ground.

"Home," Gabu said, with a sincerity that he didn't attempt to qualify.

"Yes," Daru agreed. He was quiet for a moment. Then: "Gabu."

"Mm?"

Daru opened his mouth. Nestled in his cheek, somewhat miraculously, was a single yellow berry — saved somehow through everything that had happened.

"We have a story," he said. "And a snack."
