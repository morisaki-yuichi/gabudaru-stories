The door had always been there. This was the part that stayed with Dango — not the discovery itself, but what the discovery implied: that something can be present and invisible for years, occupying the same space as your daily life without once declaring itself, and then one morning a tendril of ivy falls away and there it is, exactly where it always was, waiting with the patience of things that don't require acknowledgement to exist.

He had walked past it thousands of times. He had memorised everything within twenty metres of it. He had, at some point, classified the far corner of the garden as fully known, and in doing so had stopped looking at it — which is the particular blindness of the familiar, and which the garden's wall, in its patient grey way, had been exploiting for years.

The ivy had come loose overnight. Underneath it: wood, silver with age, iron hinges worn smooth by weather rather than use, and a latch that turned without resistance and opened onto a narrow passage that smelled of damp stone and, beyond it, something larger.

He pushed it. It swung open. He stood in the passage and breathed in what was on the other side. Then he ran back to report.

Gabu said "I know that door" with the tone of someone for whom the revelation of a secret is simply the natural conclusion of its having been kept — no drama, no apology, just: now you know.

"How long have you known?""Always.""Before us?""Before all of you.""Why didn't you say?"

Gabu considered the question with the care he gave things that deserved it. "You were happy in the garden," he said finally. "The door leads somewhere worth going, but only when you're ready to come back from it. I wasn't certain, until recently, that the distinction was clear."

Daru said: "We're going through. Now. Come on."

"Yes," said Gabu, standing. "We are."


## The Door


Gabu went last. He stood in the narrow passage — the garden visible behind him, the meadow opening ahead — and took a moment that none of the others were watching closely enough to see: a long look back at the stone and the porch and the afternoon light on the grass, which from this angle had the quality of something remembered rather than something present.

Then he stepped through.

The meadow was large in the way that absence of walls makes things large — it was simply the sky and the grass and the distance, which the garden had always implied was out there but had never made entirely real. The smell was the garden's smell with the walls removed: wilder, cooler, undirected, carrying information about rain and soil and water somewhere far away.

Dango ran. This was expected and was, in its way, necessary — the meadow was exactly the kind of space that Dango's body understood as an invitation, and refusing it would have been a kind of dishonesty. He ran through the long grass and over a rise and out of sight, and the others walked, and the gap between them was comfortable because they had long since accepted that it was the right gap for what each of them was.

He stopped at the fork because the fork presented a question his garden knowledge couldn't answer. He stood there with the unfamiliar experience of not knowing which way, and found it was less terrible than expected — more interesting, in fact, like a door of a different kind.

He sat down and waited.This, too, was new: choosing to wait instead of choosing a direction and committing to it with the confidence that works in a known space and fails in an unknown one.He was learning things about himself that the garden had never had reason to teach him.


## Into the Open


Suke-san read the fork with the methodical attention he brought to problems: sun angle, wind direction, the differential wear on the two paths, the faint persistent sound of water that entered the calculation at the last moment and settled it.

"Right," he said. He said it with confidence, which was accurate in its conclusion and slightly misleading about the certainty of its process. Gabu confirmed it, and Suke-san nodded in the way he nodded when he had been right — as though he had expected to be, which was sometimes true and sometimes performance.

Dango noticed. He noticed most things about people, which was not the same as analysing them — he simply paid attention without agenda, and things lodged. The gap between Suke-san's confident nod and the moment of genuine uncertainty that preceded it: he kept this without judgment, the way you keep an interesting stone.

The right path took them into old growth — trees that predated the garden, predated probably most things any of them had seen, their roots having long conversations with the earth that emerged in great arching curves above the surface. The light through the canopy was a different quality from garden light: more variable, less certain, arriving in moving patches rather than the steady warmth of an open sky.

Dango walked. He did not run, or rather: he ran for a while and then found himself walking, without quite deciding to, as though the terrain had revised its own terms without consulting him. The sounds were different — more species, more complexity, more things happening in the undergrowth that he couldn't see and therefore couldn't categorise and therefore had to simply leave as: unknown, and interesting.

He was moving through a space that didn't know him, which was the complementary experience to knowing a space that didn't move. Both were real. Both were, he was beginning to think, necessary.

The sound of water became unambiguous.


## The River


Wide, fast, continuously bright where the sun caught it — the river was doing several things at once in the way that rivers do, all of them in full view and none of them requiring an audience. A heron stood in the current with the absolute economy of something that has learned to make stillness into a tool. Small fish moved in the shallows with the purposefulness of things that know exactly what they're looking for.

Daru walked to the bank without speaking — which was, for Daru, its own form of eloquence — and sat down and gave the river his complete attention. He stayed that way for longer than he stayed anywhere. The river apparently merited it.

Gabu walked to the water's edge. He stood with his old feet at the margin where wet stone became dry, looking across at the far bank and the trees beyond it and, past those, at a distance he was the only one there old enough to have ever actually seen.

"You came here to decide," Suke-san said. He said it quietly, not pressing — offering a space rather than demanding a confirmation."I came here when I was uncertain," Gabu said. "This was before the garden. I was looking for somewhere to settle, which is a different kind of searching than looking for somewhere to go.""And you sat here.""I sat here for several days. The river is good for thinking — it keeps moving so you don't have to, and it makes enough sound that your thoughts have something to push against.""What did you conclude?""Nothing, while I was here," Gabu said. "I went back to the garden — it wasn't mine yet, just a garden with a wall and a door — and I started making it mine. The conclusion arrived sometime later, through the process of acting as though it were already made."

Suke-san stood beside him and looked at the river, which was indifferent to all of this in the way that useful things often are.

"The door," Suke-san said. "You kept it. You could have told us years ago.""I could have," Gabu said. "But knowing a door exists and being ready to go through it are different things. I didn't want to open it too soon.""And now?"Gabu turned to look at the group — at Dango in the water, at Daru with his pebble, at the meadow they had crossed and would cross again. "Now you know how to come back," he said. "That's what makes it safe to leave."

Dango found that the river was cold in a way that redefined cold. He stayed in it anyway, because the cold was not the point — the cold was what the river actually was, and refusing it would have been like refusing to be where he was. Daru found the pebble: grey, smooth, with one white vein of quartz running through it like a thought. He held it for a while, and then set it carefully on a flat stone above the waterline — present but not taken, which is its own kind of having.

The afternoon held them there generously.


## The Long Way Back


Returning paths have a different character from outgoing ones. The same features — the same trees, the same curve in the path, the same quality of light — present themselves from behind and require re-identification. What was background becomes foreground. What you walked through without looking at becomes the thing you're trying to recognise. Familiarity, it turns out, is directional.

Dango walked with the group this time, not ahead, which gave him a different kind of information — slower, more textured, oriented toward the shapes of things rather than their sequence. He noticed that the path had been doing things at its edges that he had moved too fast to see on the way out.

At the fork, he stopped and thought — deliberately, from principles rather than instinct, the way Suke-san did — and arrived at left through the logic of the flowers: right going out, left coming back, corroborated by the changed position of the sun.

"Left," he said."Show the working," Suke-san said — not challenging, genuinely curious."Flowers on my right going out. Reversed coming back. Sun was to our right going out, it's to our left now. Both say left."

Suke-san checked both. He nodded — a different nod than usual: considered, without the performance of certainty. "Yes. Both correct."

Dango noted this. The nod was more honest than the usual one, and more valuable for it.

The passage. The door. The click of the latch. On the other side: the garden, unchanged, which was exactly what it should be.
