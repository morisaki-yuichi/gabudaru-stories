Every morning, Dango walked around the whole garden. He knew every corner, every tree, every stone. He had done this for years.

One morning in early spring, behind the oldest tree at the very far end of the garden, he saw something he had never noticed before.

A door. In the stone wall. It was old and small and almost completely covered with green ivy. If you were not looking, you would never see it.

Dango pushed it gently with his nose. It opened.

He ran back to the others as fast as his short legs would carry him. "There is a door!" he said, out of breath. "In the wall! At the far end! I never knew it was there!"

Gabu looked up slowly from his morning spot in the sun. "Yes," he said. "I know that door."

Dango stared. "You know it?"

"Yes.""Why didn't you ever say?""You never asked," Gabu said simply.

Daru was already on his feet — or rather, already moving, which was his version of being on his feet. "Can we go through? Can we see what is out there?"

Gabu was quiet for a moment. He looked at the garden, at the stone, at each of them. Then: "Yes. I think it is time."


## The Door


They stood at the door together. It came up to Suke-san's chest. He had to lower his head to go through.

Beyond it was a narrow path between high stone walls, and then the path opened — and opened — and kept opening — into a wide green meadow that went further than the garden ever had. The sky was very large up there. The smell was different: grass and mud and something cool and moving.

There were no walls. No familiar paths. No blue stone.

Dango stood very still for one moment, taking it all in. Then he began to run.

"DANGO," Suke-san called. "Stay close!"

But Dango was already deep in the long grass, his orange-and-white shape bobbing above it like something swimming. He ran through a patch of yellow wildflowers, past a large grey rock, down a small slope — and then he stopped.

The path split in two. Left or right. He had absolutely no idea which way to go.

In the garden, he always knew. Every path led somewhere he understood. Here, he did not understand anything. He sat at the fork and waited for the others.

He had never had to wait for someone else to tell him which way to go.It was a strange feeling.He did not entirely dislike it.


## Into the Open


When the others reached him, Suke-san studied the fork carefully. He looked left. He looked right. He tipped his large ears forward and listened.

"I can hear water," he said. "Faint, but there. If the sound is coming from that direction, and the wind is coming from—" He paused. "Right," he said. "We go right."

"There is a river," Gabu said, from behind them. "Right is correct."

Suke-san nodded, as if he had known all along. But Dango had noticed the moment of uncertainty, and filed it away without saying anything.

They went right. The path wound through tall trees that were different from the ones in the garden — older, thicker, with roots that rose out of the ground and curved back in like arches. The light came through the leaves in moving patches.

More birds here than in the garden. More sounds. More movement in the undergrowth. Dango's head turned constantly.

Then they heard the water — clear and fast, getting louder as they walked.


## The River


The river was wide and bright. It moved fast over smooth round stones, making a sound that was very different from any sound in the garden. Small silver fish caught the light in the shallows. A tall grey bird — a heron — stood perfectly still in the water, watching for fish with complete patience.

Daru made a sound that was almost not a word. Then he walked to the bank and sat down and looked at the river with his full attention, which was something he very rarely gave anything.

Gabu walked to the water's edge. He stood there quietly, looking out across to the far bank.

"You have been here before," Suke-san said."Yes," Gabu said. "A long time ago. Before any of you knew this garden."

"What did you do here?" Dango asked."I sat. I watched the river. I thought.""About what?""Whether to stay in the garden. Whether it was the right place for me."

Dango looked at the garden wall, far away now, with the door in it. "And you decided to stay," he said.

"I decided to go back first," Gabu said. "Then I stayed. There is a difference."

They stayed by the river for a long time. Dango waded in up to his knees and was immediately surprised by how cold it was. Daru found a small pebble in the shallows — grey, smooth, with a single white line running through it like a crack filled with milk.

"Can I keep it?" he asked Gabu."It is not mine to give or keep," Gabu said. "It belongs to the river.""Then I will leave it here," Daru said. "But I will remember it." He set it carefully on a flat stone above the waterline, where it would not wash away.


## The Long Way Back


When the sun moved behind the trees, Gabu stood. "It is time to go back," he said.

Going back was different from going out. The paths looked different from the other direction. The big trees cast different shadows. More than once, Dango thought they had taken a wrong turn.

But this time, he did not run ahead. He walked with the group.

When they reached the fork, Dango stopped. "Left," he said, before anyone else spoke.

Suke-san looked at him. "How did you know?"

"I remembered the yellow flowers," Dango said. "We passed them on the right going out, which means they should be on the left going back."

They went left. The yellow flowers were there.

Suke-san said nothing, but something in his face shifted slightly.It was the expression of someone updating a calculation.

They came out through the narrow path and back through the small door. The garden was waiting on the other side, exactly as they had left it — the blue stone, the old porch, the familiar trees.

And yet.
