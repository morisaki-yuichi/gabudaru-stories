Dango had walked the garden's perimeter nearly every morning for as long as he had lived there. He knew it the way you know the inside of your own mouth — without thinking, by presence, by the absence of surprise. Which was why the door was startling: not because it appeared, but because it had always been there, and he had never seen it.

It was at the far end of the garden, behind the oldest tree, invisible under so many years of ivy that the wall and the door had become one object in his mind — simply: wall. A loose tendril had fallen overnight, and underneath it: wood, worn silver-grey by decades of weather, with iron hinges and an old latch that gave smoothly when he pressed it.

The door swung open. Beyond it, a narrow passage and then — open space, and the smell of water and grass and something wilder than the garden had ever offered.

He ran back at full speed and reported it, and Gabu said "I know that door" with a calm that suggested either that nothing surprised him, or that this particular thing had simply been waiting for its time.

"For how long have you known?" Dango asked."Since before you arrived," Gabu said. "Since before Suke-san. Since before Daru, though not by much.""Why didn't you say?""You were happy in the garden," Gabu said. "You didn't need the door. Until perhaps now."

Daru was already moving. "We're going through. Come on. Right now."

Gabu stood slowly. "Yes," he said. "I think we should."


## The Door


From the garden side, the door was invisible. From the outside — once you had passed through the narrow passage and turned to look back — it was small: a modest wooden door in a long stone wall, with a tree leaning over it on one side and the pale green of new ivy beginning on the other.

Gabu was last through, and he paused in the passage to look back at the garden before stepping out. What he saw — the familiar porch, the blue stone, the afternoon light on the grass — looked, from this angle, like something in a memory.

Then he stepped through, and the meadow opened up around them.

It was large in a way the garden was not. The sky occupied more of the view. The grass was longer, unmown, moving in the breeze. There were sounds the garden didn't have: a different register of birds, a constant low rustling from the undergrowth, and underneath everything, faint but unmistakeable, the sound of moving water.

Dango ran — which was expected — and disappeared into the long grass before anyone could say anything. His path was visible for a moment as a moving disturbance in the grass, and then he was over a rise and gone.

Suke-san tracked him with his eyes. "He'll stop," he said, with the calm of someone who believed it but was also ready to be wrong.

Dango stopped at the fork. He stood there with his ears forward, looking from one path to the other, for the first time in recent memory without a clear answer to a clear question. He waited. The not-knowing settled over him like something he hadn't worn before — a little tight at first, and then simply present.


## Into the Open


Suke-san reached the fork and read it carefully: the angle of the afternoon sun, the wind direction, the way the grass was marginally more worn on the right-hand path than the left. He could hear water — thin and distant, but real — from the right.

"Right," he said. "The water's to the right. Based on the sun position, we're facing roughly east, which puts the sound coming from the northeast. The right path goes that way.""Right is correct," Gabu confirmed.

Suke-san nodded, with the composure of someone who had just made a confident guess and was relieved to have it confirmed. Dango noted the gap between the confidence and the relief, and said nothing — but he thought about it.

The right path wound through old growth, trees much older than the garden's: wide trunks, roots arching out of the earth, a canopy so thick that the light came through in moving patches. Everything here had a different scale. The sounds were richer: more species of birds, more activity in the undergrowth, the dry rustle of things Dango couldn't see.

He found himself walking rather than running, which was not entirely a conscious choice. The terrain had changed, and with it, without quite meaning to, he had changed too — more attentive, more uncertain in the good way, moving as someone in a new place rather than someone on a familiar circuit.

The sound of water grew until it was no longer a question.


## The River


The river was wide and fast, moving over smooth stones with a sound that managed to be simultaneously urgent and peaceful. It caught the afternoon sun in constantly shifting pieces. A heron stood in the current with the absolute stillness of someone for whom the world is sufficiently explained by what is in front of them right now.

Daru walked to the bank without saying anything and sat down facing the water and just stayed there. This was remarkable enough — Daru staying anywhere without talking — that no one mentioned it.

Gabu walked slowly to the edge and stood looking out across to the far bank. Something in his posture was different from the garden: more open, perhaps, or more private. He had a history with this place that none of the others shared.

"You've been here many times," Suke-san said quietly, standing beside him."A few. The first time was before the garden. I was trying to decide where to settle.""And you sat here and decided?""I sat here and stopped moving," Gabu said. "The deciding happened gradually, over the following year or so. I went back to the garden, which was not yet mine, and I began to make it mine. By the time I had finished making it mine, the decision was already made.""That's an unusual way to decide something.""Most important decisions work that way," Gabu said. "You think you're choosing, but actually you're already in the middle of living the choice."

Dango waded in — gasped — stayed — and gradually worked out that the cold was, in fact, wonderful. The stones underfoot were smooth in a way the garden's path never was. Small fish investigated him briefly and left.

Daru found the pebble: grey, smooth, with one white line through it like a seam of quartz. He held it for a while, turning it over, and then put it on a flat stone above the waterline.

"Belonging to the river?" Suke-san asked."Belonging to the river," Daru confirmed. "But I'll know it's here."

The afternoon moved around them at the river's pace, which was generous with time.


## The Long Way Back


The return trip taught Dango something about how much of his sense of direction was really a sense of familiarity. The same paths, seen from the other end, were genuinely different — not threatening, but genuinely unfamiliar in ways that required actual attention rather than the fluid, automatic navigation of known territory.

He didn't run ahead. He walked with the group, which meant at Gabu's pace, and discovered that Gabu's pace left enough time to look at things.

At the fork, he stopped and thought — deliberately and from scratch — rather than running to the front and waiting. The yellow flowers had been on his right going out. Coming back, they should be on his left. He checked where the sun was. The geometry held.

"Left," he said.Suke-san looked at him. "Show your working.""The flowers were on my right when we came out. So they should be on my left going back. And the sun's moved — it was to our right going out, it should be to our left now."Suke-san checked. "Yes. That's right."

He said it in the tone of someone for whom being right on someone else's reasoning was a new and slightly uncomfortable experience. Dango noticed this too, and again said nothing about it.

Through the passage, and back through the door. The latch settled with a quiet click. The garden lay before them, unchanged.
