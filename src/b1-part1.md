Gabu and Daru had been sharing the same garden for as long as either of them could remember, and in that time they had learned — more or less — how to be friends despite having almost nothing in common. Gabu was large and deliberate, the kind of tortoise who paused before every decision, however small. Not because he was slow-minded, but because he had discovered that patience was almost always the most efficient approach in the end. Daru, smaller and faster, operated almost entirely on instinct and enthusiasm, which led to results that were sometimes brilliant and sometimes predictable in the wrong direction.


## A Hungry Morning


On the morning in question, Daru had woken before the garden had properly decided to be daytime. He lay still for a moment, letting his senses gather themselves — and then he sat up, because something had arrived on the breeze that wasn't usually there.

It was warm, sweet, and insisted upon being noticed.

"Gabu," he said, pressing against his friend. "You need to wake up. Something is happening."

Gabu had been in the middle of a perfectly comfortable sleep, but he had long since learned to distinguish between Daru's various tones of urgency. This one was genuine.

"Good morning," he said carefully. "What seems to be the matter?"

"Tomatoes. The big ones near the old tree — I'd recognise that smell anywhere. They're at their best right now. If we don't go soon, the whole morning will be wasted."

Gabu considered this. He looked at the sky, which was clear and unhurried. "All right," he said. "But we go together, and we watch the path. It rained last night."


## The Long Walk


Daru, predictably, went ahead. Despite his best intentions, he found it impossible to hold back when his legs were telling him to move, and so a pattern developed: he would get thirty metres ahead, realise he was alone, circle back, and repeat. He was in the middle of this cycle when he failed to notice that the edge of the path had crumbled slightly overnight.

One moment he was moving; the next he was on his side against a large brown rock, unable to get up. The angle was entirely wrong and he knew from experience that he couldn't right himself unaided.

"Gabu!" he called, keeping his voice as steady as he could. "I could use some help here!"

Gabu had been watching from a distance and had been on the point of calling out a warning. He arrived without comment.


## Gabu to the Rescue


He examined the situation for a moment — the angle, the ground, the best approach — and then positioned himself carefully and pushed with steady, deliberate force.

One, two, three... push!

It took three attempts. Then Daru was upright, a little winded but otherwise unhurt.

"I was about to warn you about that part of the path," Gabu said. "I should have said something sooner. I'm sorry."

Daru blinked. He had expected at least a mild "I told you so." "Don't apologise. I wasn't paying attention. It's my own fault." He paused. "Thank you."

"From now on, we walk the whole way together," Gabu said. "Even if it takes longer. Especially if it takes longer."


## The Sweet Reward


They walked side by side for the rest of the journey, which was slower but, Daru had to admit, considerably more pleasant. When they finally arrived at the old tree, they found exactly what Daru had promised — five tomatoes, heavy and deeply red, lying in the cool shade where the grass grew long.

They ate without rushing. Chomp, chomp, chomp. The tomatoes had the particular sweetness of fruit that has been allowed to ripen in its own time, and the taste was better for having been anticipated so keenly.

"I'm glad we came," Daru said.

"So am I," Gabu said. He glanced sideways at his friend. "I usually am, with you."
