What Daru wanted, fundamentally, was to know what was on the other side of things. The fence. The big tree's canopy. The far corner of the garden he hadn't quite reached yet. This impulse was not recklessness — or at least, he had never considered it as such. It was, more accurately, a kind of faith: a belief that the world held more than what was currently visible, and that the correct response to that belief was to go and look.

Gabu did not share this faith, or rather, he held it in tension with a competing conviction: that what you already had was worth protecting. They had argued this, implicitly, for as long as they had been friends, and neither had yet persuaded the other. Today the argument was going to be conducted at some volume, and in rain.


## The Strange Noise


Gabu was asleep when Daru found the hole. He had been drawn to the fence by a sound — scritch, scratch, scritch — that was too rhythmic to be wind and too quiet to be anything large. The hole was at the very base of the fence, half-concealed by long grass that had grown into the gap. It was barely wide enough for a tortoise.

Daru pressed his face against it and saw, on the other side, something that arranged itself immediately in his mind as beautiful: tall purple flowers nodding in a private breeze, grass that grew without the constraint of a garden's purpose, and a dense, chaotic bush encrusted with clusters of yellow berries the colour of very old sunshine. It looked, in a word, unexplored.

"Gabu," he called, loud enough to carry across the garden. "You need to come and look at this. I mean it."

Gabu came, slowly and with the wariness of someone who has learned that "you need to come and look at this" has historically preceded the more interesting episodes of his life. He looked through the gap for a long moment. Then he looked at the sky.

The sky was the colour of pewter and moving purposefully.

"I see what you're thinking," he said.

"It would be quick — half an hour. The flowers, the berries, back through before anything happens. Gabu, when will we get another chance like this?"

"The sky looks like a storm that has already made its decision," Gabu said. He paused. Then: "You'll go whether I come or not, won't you."

Daru didn't answer. He didn't need to.

"Then I'm coming," Gabu said, "and we leave the moment I say so. Not when you're ready. The moment I say."


## Into the Wild


The other side of the fence had a quality that gardens, by definition, lack: it was indifferent to them. The grass grew tall because it grew tall, not to accommodate anyone's height. The air smelled of something complicated — earth and rain and something flowering that had no obligation to be pleasant, but happened to be. It was wild in the specific sense of being entirely itself.

The yellow berries tasted of something Daru didn't have a word for — warm and slightly sharp, like joy with an edge to it. He ate three before he could stop himself.

"Gabu. Try one."

Gabu did. He stood still for a moment with the taste of it. Then he looked up at the sky, which had darkened by approximately one full shade since they had come through.

"We need to go now," he said. "I'm not being cautious. I'm being accurate."

The thunder arrived before Daru had formulated a response. Not a warning rumble but a full, decisive BOOM that resonated in the earth beneath them. And then the rain: not the tentative beginning of a shower but a complete commitment, as though the sky had simply decided to become water.


## Lost in the Rain


The world reorganised itself in the rain. The tall grass, which had been a gentle labyrinth in the dry afternoon, became something denser and less navigable. Landmarks disappeared. Directions became suggestions. The sound of the storm — the drumming on leaves, the rush of water over root and rock — made it difficult to think clearly, which was, Daru recognised with a feeling not unlike dread, precisely the worst time to need to think clearly.

"The fence is — it was—" He turned around twice, which told him nothing except that he had turned around twice. "Gabu, I don't know which direction."

"Neither do I," Gabu said steadily. "Stay still. Moving randomly makes it worse."

Water was running along the ground now in determined little rivers, and Daru felt it pushing at his feet. He was lighter than Gabu; the current found him first. He tried to compensate, misjudged, and went sliding towards a low pool that had not existed twenty minutes ago.

"Gabu—"

Gabu had already identified the tree root — a thick arch of wood that rose from the mud like a natural shelter, a few metres to the right. He had been calculating the angle since the first wave of water came. "To your right — the root, now, run!"

Daru ran. He got under the root and turned immediately, arms out. Gabu came last, slipping on the final approach, and Daru held on until they were both safe in the small, dark, damp hollow that had somehow materialised exactly when they needed it.


## Waiting it Out


The hollow smelled of old wood and wet soil. Outside, the storm was conducting its business with no apparent interest in their welfare. Inside, there was enough room for two tortoises who were prepared to sit close together, which they were.

Daru said: "I'm sorry."

"I know."

"I thought we'd have time."

"I know that too." Gabu was quiet for a moment. "You know what I keep thinking about? The berries. That one I ate. I keep thinking it was worth it — which probably means my judgment is also flawed, just in a different direction."

Daru looked at him. "You're not angry with me."

"I chose to follow you," Gabu said. "That means I own part of this. We got into it together and we'll get out of it together, which is, if you think about it, how most things are supposed to work."

They waited. They talked about warmth and food and the dry parts of the garden. They talked about what they would do first when they got home. Outside, the storm took its time.


## The Way Home


Storms end. This one did. The rain diminished by degrees until what remained was less a storm than a memory of one — a dripping, glinting, newly-washed stillness that had its own kind of beauty. The flattened grass revealed the fence exactly where it had always been, patient and unimpressed.

They walked home without hurrying, stepping with care over the mud and the small rivers that were already deciding where to go. When they came through the gap and found themselves back in the familiar garden, something in both of them loosened that they hadn't known was tight.

"Home," Gabu said. The word was plain and sufficient.

"Home," Daru agreed. Then he opened his mouth, and there, improbably salvaged from the entire afternoon, was a yellow berry. He had held it, without thinking about it, the whole time.

"We have a story," he said. "And a snack."

Gabu looked at the berry for a moment. "I hope it's the same kind I had before," he said.

It was.
