Spring had settled into the garden, and with it came a restless, expanding sort of energy — the kind that makes things happen whether you intend them to or not. Gabu and Daru were both a little bigger than they had been. They were now, if you measured carefully, the same size, which Daru mentioned regularly and which Gabu received without comment.

After the storm beyond the fence, Gabu had developed an even greater appreciation for the familiar and predictable. He preferred the known to the unknown. Daru, meanwhile, remained on the lookout for whatever came next. They were, as ever, a well-matched pair — and they were about to meet their third.


## The Arrival of Dango


The garden had been quiet all morning. Gabu and Daru were eating dandelion leaves near the old porch. Then, without any transition, it was not quiet. The ground vibrated. A large, orange-and-white energy arrived around the corner of the house, barking at full volume.

"Woof! Woof! Thump, thump, thump!"

It was a Corgi puppy — specifically, the neighbour's new Corgi, whose name turned out to be Dango. He had a long body, very short legs, ears that were still working out their final size, and a tail that appeared to be generating its own electricity. He approached at full speed and at extremely close range.

"Hello! Hello! Are you rocks? You look like rocks but you're moving — are you animals? What kind? Are you friendly? Can you run? Do you like digging?"

Gabu withdrew slightly. "I'm not a rock," he said, with considerable patience. "My name is Gabu. If you could lower your voice by about half, that would be helpful."

Daru, meanwhile, had stretched his neck forward with undisguised fascination. "I'm Daru! What are you? Your legs are extremely short."

"I'm Dango! My legs are normal! Well — they're short but they're very fast. Watch!"

He demonstrated by running three tight circles around Gabu in the time it took Gabu to take one step. Daru found this delightful. Gabu found it exhausting to watch.

Despite the volume and the velocity, however, both tortoises could see that Dango was fundamentally kind — the sort of creature who means well so completely that you find yourself forgiving the inconveniences. Over the weeks that followed, he became part of the garden's daily life: loud, enthusiastic, occasionally chaotic, and entirely indispensable.


## A Terrible Loss


The trouble began, as trouble often does, on the hottest afternoon of the season.

Gabu and Daru had found refuge in the cool, damp earth near the compost heap. It was the best spot in the garden when the sun was at its most uncompromising, and they had retreated there gratefully. Dango had come to visit, as he did every day, and had immediately begun digging a hole nearby with the single-minded dedication that was his signature.

"Look at the depth on this one," he announced.

"It's very impressive," Daru said. "Gabu, don't you think it's impressive?"

"I think the flying dirt is getting in my eye," Gabu said, moving a fraction to the left.

The heat eventually did what heat does, and all three of them fell asleep in the afternoon quiet.

When Daru woke up in the early evening, the light had gone golden and Gabu was still sleeping. Daru looked at his friend with the particular fondness one feels for sleeping companions — and then he noticed something wrong.

A month ago, Gabu had found a small, smooth blue stone near the water bowl. It had a quality about it — a depth to its colour, a pleasing weight — that Gabu had found appealing. He had spent an afternoon fixing it to the centre of his shell with tree sap, and he had worn it since with understated pride. It had, in a quiet way, become part of who he was.

It was gone. Just a faint pale oval where it had rested.

Gabu woke up a few minutes later to find Daru examining his shell with a very specific expression.

"Daru. What are you doing?"

"Gabu," Daru said carefully, "I need to tell you something about your stone."


## The Search Party


Dango had been resting in the shade nearby and woke at the sound of their voices. He bounded over with his customary readiness.

"What's happening? Is there a mystery? I'm excellent at mysteries."

"My blue stone has fallen off," Gabu said. He said it calmly, but both Daru and Dango could hear something underneath the calm. "It could be anywhere between here and where I was sleeping earlier."

Dango's entire manner changed. The tail slowed to a thoughtful wag. He tapped his nose with one paw. "Leave this to me. I can find anything with this nose. It's not a boast — it's just what noses are for."

They searched the area thoroughly. Dango worked in slow, systematic loops with his face close to the earth. Gabu and Daru moved through the grass in careful grids. They searched near the compost heap, under every large leaf, around the water bowl, along the fence line. The light faded. Nothing.

It was Daru who looked at Dango's hole last.

"Dango," he said. "Your digging this afternoon — there was a lot of flying dirt."

Dango went very still. His ears went down. "Oh no," he said quietly. "Oh no, oh no, oh no."

He ran to the hole and began to dig again — but differently this time. Slowly. Carefully. Moving thin layers of earth aside with the delicacy of someone who understood the stakes. Gabu and Daru watched without speaking.

Then Dango stopped. He lowered his nose to a particular point and held it there. He nudged the earth aside with exceptional gentleness.

A small, deep blue light appeared in the dark soil.

"Found it," Dango whispered.


## True Friendship


Daru retrieved the stone and brought it to Gabu. But the old sap had broken down completely. The stone wouldn't stay on Gabu's shell any longer without being re-fixed — and both of them could tell from looking at the pale oval that the shell's surface had changed slightly while the stone had been away.

Gabu held the stone in his mouth for a long moment. He looked at Dango, who was sitting very still with an expression of genuine remorse. Then he looked at Daru, and something in his face shifted — the look of someone who has arrived at a decision not through reasoning but through feeling.

"Dango," he said. "You didn't mean for this to happen. And you found it. You found it with your nose, in the dark, on the first try. You're a better detective than you give yourself credit for."

"I buried it in the first place," Dango said.

"You did," Gabu agreed. "And now I have something to tell you both." He set the stone on the ground in front of him and considered it. "I've been thinking, while you were searching, about what this stone is for. I put it on my shell because I liked it. But I think I like this more: that all three of us looked for it together. That you found it, Dango. That it brought us here."

He picked up the stone and walked to the entrance of the space under the porch where he and Daru slept. He set it down just inside the threshold.

"This belongs to all of us," he said. "It's at the door, so we'll all see it every day."

Daru's eyes went very bright. Dango's tail resumed full speed.
