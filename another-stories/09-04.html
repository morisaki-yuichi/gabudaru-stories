September 4

The Swallows Leave

🐢 Gabu🐢 Daru🐕 Dango

Every summer, swallows nested under the eaves of the house.

Gabu had watched them arrive in spring — small, fast birds with pointed wings and forked tails. They made nests of mud and grass. They raised their young through the summer.

Now, in September, they were leaving.

Gabu watched them gather on the wire above the garden wall. Fifteen, twenty, thirty birds. They sat very close together. They called to each other in thin, sharp voices.

Daru and Dango came and stood beside him.

"Where are they going?" Dango said.

"South," said Gabu. "Very far south. To warmer places."

Dango stared at the birds. They seemed ordinary from far away. But they were about to fly thousands of miles.

"Do they know the way?" Daru said.

"They know," Gabu said. "It is inside them. They do not need a map."

As they watched, the birds suddenly lifted — all at once, as if pulled by a single thought. They rose in a wide arc over the garden and turned south.

They were gone in seconds.

Dango watched the empty wire.

"They will come back?" it said.

"In spring," Gabu said. "They always come back."

"The same ones?" Dango said.

Gabu thought. "Some of the same ones," he said. "And their young, born this summer."

Dango watched the sky where the swallows had been.

"We will watch for them in spring," it said.

"Yes," said Gabu. "We will."
