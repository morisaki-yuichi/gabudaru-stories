Suke-san Does Not Need Anyone

The garden had a new neighbour, and the first thing he did upon arrival was establish, clearly and privately, the terms of his residency: he would observe; he would not impose; he would not, under any circumstances, become dependent on the company of others. He had considered this carefully, and the logic was sound. Other creatures were unpredictable. Attachment was a form of vulnerability. He was a Shiba Inu, which carried with it certain expectations about dignity and self-sufficiency, and he intended to meet them.

His name was Suke-san. He was a puppy, though he carried his puppyhood with conspicuous discomfort, as though it were a temporary condition he was actively working to move through. His ears were still too large. His paws were still too large. He had decided to simply not think about either of these facts.

He walked the garden wall on his first morning with his chin elevated and his gaze composed, and surveyed his new territory with appropriate detachment. The flowers were acceptable. The trees were adequate. The clouds were doing what clouds do.

I am completely fine, he thought. I have everything I need. This is sufficient.

A butterfly passed close to his face — close enough that he felt the miniature current of its wings — and he watched it longer than he intended to, and without meaning to at all, his tail described one small arc.

He arrested it immediately. He looked around to confirm that no one had been watching.

Good. No witnesses. The record remained clean.

The First Morning

Dango arrived the way Dango arrived everywhere — at full speed, with his entire body participating in his enthusiasm, as though the concept of reserve were simply a language he had never been taught. He had spotted Suke-san from the far end of the garden and covered the distance between them in what appeared to be no time at all.

Suke-san arranged himself quickly into an attitude of dignified composure.

"Good morning," he said, with a small, precise bow. "I am Suke-san."

"I'm Dango! I know everyone here, come and meet them, you're going to love them, come on, right now!"

Suke-san looked at him — the spinning tail, the bright, bottomless eyes, the entire architecture of a creature for whom the concept of restraint was theoretical — and followed. He told himself this was merely a reconnaissance exercise. He told himself it very firmly.

The two tortoises were in the morning light near the porch, the blue stone at their door catching the sun in a way that was, Suke-san noticed, out of proportion to its size. One of them was examining a leaf with the unhurried concentration of someone who had nowhere better to be. The other spotted Suke-san and turned around so fast that Suke-san instinctively stepped back.

"Hi! I'm Daru! Are you new? What do you like? Have you noticed the fat bird in the tree? He's round like a ball — come look, he's in the second branch—"

"Hello," said the other one, looking up from his leaf. "I'm Gabu. Welcome to the garden." And then he returned to his leaf.

Just like that. No performance. No continued attention. No attempt to make an impression.

Suke-san found this unexpectedly affecting. He stood very still for a moment, studying Gabu, who was simply there — present, contained, entirely self-sufficient, doing precisely what he wanted to do and apparently requiring nothing from anyone watching. It was a quality Suke-san had constructed for himself as a philosophy, and here it was, in an elderly tortoise, practiced without effort.

He noted this. He filed it away under a category he hadn't yet named.

"It was good to meet you," he said. He turned and walked back to the wall.

He thought about the stone for a long time that afternoon.

The Rainy Afternoon

He maintained his independence for three days with some success. The wall was his, the mornings were his, the quiet circuits of the garden boundary were his. He watched the others from a distance — Gabu's unhurried progress through the grass, Daru's constant trajectory toward whatever had most recently caught his attention, Dango's perpetual motion — and told himself this was sufficient. Observation was its own form of connection. It asked nothing of you.

The storm arrived on the fourth day with the particular authority of weather that has made up its mind. Suke-san was on the wall when the first drops fell. He was on the ground, under a tree, when the first tree proved permeable. He was under a second tree when the thunder arrived: a low, sustained BOOM that seemed to live in the earth as much as the air, and which he felt in his paws before he heard it.

His legs went rigid. His ears pressed flat against his skull. His eyes moved very quickly in all directions.

He was fine. This was objectively fine. He was a Shiba Inu. Shiba Inus are not unsettled by weather. He was simply reassessing his position in light of new information.

The second thunderclap made the decision for him. His paws selected a direction entirely independently of his will, and he found himself, some seconds later, standing in front of the porch with the blue stone at his feet, his fur plastered flat, the rain working hard at his dignity.

"Suke-san!" Daru appeared in the doorway, bright and apparently delighted. "Come in — we have room!"

Suke-san sat near the entrance, back straight, expression composed. He was grateful for the shelter. He was not, he was careful to note internally, grateful for anything more than the shelter.

"Were you frightened?" Daru asked, with a guilelessness that made the question impossible to deflect.
"No," Suke-san said.
"Oh, interesting. I was, a bit. I usually am." A pause. "Gabu says being honest about it makes it smaller."

Suke-san looked at him. Then at Gabu, who was watching the rain with the patient calm of someone for whom all weathers are merely weather. Then at Dango, who had apparently decided that the rain was a game that had been invented specifically for his enjoyment and was running through it with full commitment.

"He's not scared," Suke-san said.
"Of this? No," said Daru. "Of the vacuum cleaner — yes. Very much yes."

Something happened in Suke-san's chest — a small seismic event, precisely located, unmistakable in nature. A laugh, or the shape of one, or the pressure of one pressing urgently against the inside of his composure.

He held it back.
But only just.

Gabu told the story of the stone — or rather, Daru told it, with Gabu occasionally correcting a detail or adding a quiet observation that reframed everything. Finding it, fixing it to his shell, losing it to Dango's exuberance, the search in the fading light, and then Dango — Dango whose nose found it in the dark, in the earth he had moved himself — and Gabu's decision to put it at the door where it would belong to all of them.

"Why?" Suke-san asked, before he had decided to.

"Because some things are better shared," Gabu said simply, still watching the rain.

Suke-san sat with that answer for a long time. The rain continued; Daru talked; the afternoon arranged itself around the four of them in the small dry space, and Suke-san found, to his considerable surprise, that he did not want to leave. He sat with that discovery too. He sat with it carefully, the way you sit with something that might be fragile.

When the rain stopped, he stood, shook himself dry with precision, and said: "Thank you. For the shelter."

"Come back," Daru said, in the tone of someone issuing an invitation that was also, somehow, a statement of fact.
"Perhaps," said Suke-san.

He looked at the stone one more time. He walked out into the garden, which smelled of rain and green things and something very much like the beginning of something.

Behind him, completely on its own, his tail moved once.
He let it.

The Morning He Came Back

He told himself several things on the walk home, all of them reasonable. That the shelter had been practical, not symbolic. That he had simply been in the vicinity the next morning. That sitting beside Daru and watching the light move across the grass was a neutral activity, observable anywhere, with no particular meaning.

He was not sure he believed any of it, but the telling was a habit, and habits are hard to break quickly.

Gabu gave him the nod — exactly the same as before, no more, no less, without any register of surprise or satisfaction — and went to his morning spot in the sun. The nod said, clearly: you were expected when you arrived, and nothing is required of you beyond being here.

Suke-san was still trying to work out how to accept that cleanly when Dango arrived.

"YOU'RE HERE. I KNEW IT. GABU, HE'S HERE, I TOLD YOU—"

Suke-san sat up. "I was just—"

But Dango's ears were bouncing and Dango was bouncing and the garden was full of the morning and Suke-san's tail was doing the thing it did, entirely without his authorisation, the thing he had been managing and suppressing and explaining away since the first morning he walked this wall alone.

He let it.

He just — let it go.

"Daru," he said, when Dango had settled.
"Mm?"
"I'm not very good at this," Suke-san said. "At being — in a group. At any of this. I'm not sure I know how."
"That's all right," Daru said, without hesitation. "Neither was I, in different ways. Neither is Dango, in quite a lot of ways." He glanced at Gabu. "Gabu's good at it, but he had to learn too, I think."

"I did," Gabu said, without looking up from the grass. "It takes as long as it takes."

Suke-san sat beside them in the morning sun, with the blue stone at the door and Dango's warm weight gradually settling against his side, and he thought: I do not need this. I could leave at any time. I am choosing to stay, which is different from needing.

He thought this several more times over the course of the morning, with decreasing conviction.

That evening, walking home through the lengthening shadows, Suke-san assembled an accounting of the day. What had been given; what had been asked. Nothing, he concluded, had been asked of him that he had not already been prepared to give — which was perhaps the point.

He thought about Gabu's nod. He thought about Daru's voice filling the afternoon like sunlight fills a room. He thought about Dango, who was frightened of exactly one thing in the world, and who had told him so without any apparent shame.

A leaf drifted past him in the soft evening air.

He watched it fall.

His tail wagged, and he was not even looking around to check if anyone had seen, and that — that was new.

He walked home with his chin up and his tail high and the quiet, interior certainty that tomorrow morning the stone would be at the door and the garden would be there, and so would they, and so — without having planned it, without needing it, entirely on his own terms — would he.

The garden had a new neighbour.
And now — on his own terms, in his own time — he was not alone. 🐾✨
