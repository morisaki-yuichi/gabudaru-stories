The Great Tomato Adventure

There was no particular reason for this morning to announce itself as different from any other. The garden held its usual early quiet — damp with dew, the light still tentative, the air carrying the particular stillness that belongs to the hour before most creatures have committed to the day. Gabu was asleep beneath his favourite overhang of stone. Daru was not.

Daru had been awake for some minutes, doing what he habitually did in those first moments of wakefulness: lying still and letting his senses assemble a picture of the world. It was a practice he had developed not through discipline but through appetite — there was always something worth noticing, if you were quiet enough to catch it. And this morning, one element of the picture was pressing itself insistently on his attention.

Something in the air. Something warm and sweet that threaded past the smell of wet earth and new growth and would not be ignored.

A Hungry Morning

"Gabu." He pressed his nose against his friend's shell. "Gabu. I need you to be awake. This is worth waking up for."

Gabu surfaced from sleep in the manner of someone who had mastered the art of transition — not abruptly, not reluctantly, but with the steady unhurriedness that characterised everything about him. He blinked at the morning.

"What is it?" he asked. Then, smelling the air: "...Oh."

"Tomatoes," Daru said. "The ones near the old tree — I haven't been able to stop thinking about them. They're ready. I'd stake anything on it."

There was a pause while Gabu looked at the sky and then at the path ahead. "All right," he said, in the tone of someone who had long since made peace with the fact that some expeditions are inevitable. "But we go carefully, and we go together."

The Long Walk

The garden looked different before the day had fully established itself. Familiar shapes were unfamiliar in their early-morning shadows; the path that Daru normally navigated without thought seemed to have subtly rearranged itself overnight. He went ahead anyway, because going ahead was in his nature, and because part of him had always believed that the best way to find out whether a path was safe was to walk it.

He could hear Gabu behind him — measured, steady, step... step... step — and there was something in that sound that was, if he was being honest, reassuring. Like a metronome. Like something that would still be there when he looked around.

It was because he was looking ahead rather than down that he missed the crumbled edge of the path entirely. One moment he was moving; the next the world had tilted dramatically, and he was on his side against a large brown rock with his legs making useless movements in the air.

"Gabu," he said, with as much composure as he could assemble. "I've had a small incident."

Gabu to the Rescue

Gabu arrived without hurry and without commentary, which was somehow more dignified than any words could have been. He assessed the situation with the quiet thoroughness of someone who has learned that rushing an assessment only leads to a second attempt, then positioned himself at the precise angle that physics required.

One, two, three — push.

And again. And on the third push, Daru was upright, heart still clattering, tasting the iron of adrenaline and garden soil.

"Thank you," Daru said, when he had caught his breath.

"You always fall on the same side," Gabu observed, not unkindly. "I've been meaning to ask if that means something."

Daru laughed, which was perhaps not the expected response, but which released something that had been tightly coiled in his chest. "Maybe it just means I always lean slightly left." He paused. "How are you so reliably calm about everything?"

"One of us has to be," Gabu said. "And you're clearly not available for the role."

They walked the rest of the way side by side, Daru slightly more attentive to the ground, Gabu slightly closer than usual to his friend.

The Sweet Reward

The old tree received them in its customary way — cool shade, the smell of bark and deep roots and something alive that had no name. Under its wide lower branches, five tomatoes lay in the long grass, heavy with their own ripeness, their skins faintly warm even in the shade.

They ate in the particular silence that belongs to shared meals. Chomp, chomp, chomp. The tomatoes tasted the way ripe things always taste when you've been anticipating them — startlingly vivid, as though flavour were partly a function of wanting.

Afterwards they lay in the cool grass and let the morning do what mornings do. Neither of them felt any particular need to be elsewhere.

"Good morning," Daru said eventually, to no one in particular.

"Good morning," Gabu replied. He didn't add anything more.

He didn't need to.

The garden grew warm around them as they slept, side by side,
in the long shade of the old tree. 🌞
