The Long Way Round

Suke-san had a model of himself as someone who could navigate. It was not an unreasonable model — he had a good sense of direction, a systematic mind, the ability to orient by sun and shadow and the slope of the ground. In the garden and on the streets he knew, the model was accurate. The error was in the assumption that these capabilities were portable, that navigation was a general skill rather than a specific knowledge applied to a specific space, and that the market town would yield to the same approach that the garden did.

The truck, and the crowd, and the sudden absence of Gabu and Daru, were the data that began revising the model. The next hour completed the revision.

Gabu, for his part, had also been managing a model — one that included the probability of exactly this kind of separation and the most likely resolution. He was not unconcerned. He was simply operating the concern in a way that produced movement rather than paralysis, which is the thing practice teaches you and experience is the only real teacher of.

These are the two stories of that afternoon: the separated ones and the waiting ones, running in parallel, converging at a fountain in a square where the water caught the afternoon light in the way Gabu had been remembering for years.

The Separated: Suke-san and Dango

The first thing Suke-san did was stop moving. This was correct — or rather, it would have been correct if he had stopped in order to think, which is what he believed he was doing. In fact he stopped in order to reassert control over a situation in which his usual methods of control had just failed, which is a different thing that produces similar behaviour and different results.

He retraced. He had the route in his mind as a general impression rather than a specific sequence, which turned out to matter a great deal. Retracing a general impression of a route through specific streets produced, reliably, a different specific route — similar in character but wrong in detail, which in navigation is another way of saying: wrong.

After two attempts, he stopped again. This time he actually stopped — he found a spot out of the foot traffic, sat down, and ran through the set of things he actually knew, separately from the things he had assumed. The list of things he actually knew was shorter than expected. He found this useful and uncomfortable in equal measure, which is usually a sign of genuine information.

Dango was sitting beside him. He had been sitting beside him throughout, following Suke-san's navigational attempts with the same patient willingness he brought to anything Suke-san decided to do — not because he lacked views but because he understood, without needing to articulate it, that Suke-san needed the opportunity to find his own limits rather than being shown them.

Now Dango looked up. On a window ledge above them: a large grey cat of indeterminate age, watching the street with the specific quality of attention of someone for whom this view has been the primary entertainment for years.

"Excuse me," Dango said. "The big stone fountain — the one in the market square. Do you know it?"
"Know it," said the cat. "Two streets south, left at the bakery. The water sounds like rain when the wind's up."
"Thank you very much."

Suke-san watched this exchange with the expression of someone who is recalibrating. Not the cat specifically — he was aware, dimly, that he was being unfair to cats as a category — but the approach. The directness of it. The complete absence of the internal audit that would have preceded his own asking: is this a reliable source, is there a better one, is asking the best available method, is there information I could obtain independently first.

"You just asked," he said.
"Yes," said Dango. "She knows the streets."
"You assessed that from—"
"She's been sitting there a long time," Dango said. "You can tell. The comfortable way she's positioned. The way she watches without looking for anything. She's not a visitor."
Suke-san looked at the cat. The cat, indifferent to being discussed, returned to watching the street. "That's a reasonable inference," he said slowly. "I would have called it an assumption and discounted it."
"It's the same thing," Dango said. "But you trust yours more when you call it inference."

Suke-san had no immediate response to this. He filed it under: things that are correct and inconvenient.

Two streets south, left at the bakery: the fountain. They heard it before they saw it — a thin continuous sound, cleaner than the street noise, coming from an open space. The square was broad and mostly empty at this hour, the stone fountain at its centre sending water over a worn lip into a basin below.

Suke-san stood and looked at the fountain for a moment.
"The inference was right," he said.
"Yes," said Dango. "You knew which way south was. I knew who to ask. Between us we had a complete navigation."
Suke-san considered this framing. It was generous. It was also, he thought, accurate. "That's one way to look at it," he said.
"Is there another way?"
"Yes," said Suke-san. "But I think yours is more useful."

The Waiting: Gabu and Daru

Gabu had not moved dramatically after the truck. He had found a space and stood in it and thought, which from the outside looked like nothing and was, from the inside, a considerable amount of activity.

The situation was: Dango and Suke-san were separated from them in a town neither pair knew well. Both parties were capable of finding their way to the same place if they reasoned toward the same destination. The question was whether Suke-san would identify the right destination. Gabu thought about Suke-san — about his particular cast of mind, the way he moved from observation to logic to action — and concluded: yes. The fountain. He would choose the fountain.

Gabu began walking toward the square.

Daru walked beside him, keeping up, keeping the worry inside where it couldn't get in the way. "How can you do this?" he asked, not for the first time and perhaps not for the last.

"Do what?"
"Be calm. Appear calm. I can't tell if you're actually calm or just appearing to be."
"Both, in different proportions at different moments," Gabu said. "The appearance of calm is partly performance, but only partly. The rest of it is practised — I've been frightened in enough situations that I've learned to use the fear differently."
"Use it how?"
"As information. I'm afraid right now in the way that means: this matters, pay attention. Not in the way that means: I can't think. I've made that distinction enough times that it's become more automatic."

Daru thought about this. Somewhere in the market, a stall-keeper was calling out prices in a cheerful monotone. "Were you really lost once?" he asked. "Lost like this?"

"Much more lost than this. Alone, in a large unfamiliar place, for three days."
"Before the garden."
"Before the garden."
"What happened?"
"I stopped trying to retrace my steps, which weren't tracked well enough to retrace. I identified what I was certain of — the direction of the sun, the fact that water flows downhill to larger water — and I moved toward certainty instead of away from uncertainty. Different direction, different result."
"Three days," Daru said.
"The third night was uncomfortable," Gabu said, with the understatement of someone for whom the actual experience was not available in its original intensity. "But the third morning I found a stream, and the stream found a river, and the river found a road, and the road found a town I recognised."
"Were you different afterwards?"
Gabu was quiet for a moment. "I knew something I hadn't known before," he said. "Not how to navigate — I already knew that. I knew what it felt like to be genuinely uncertain about whether I would be all right. And then to be all right anyway." He paused. "It changes how afraid you can be of future uncertainties. Not because they're less real, but because you have evidence about what happens afterward."

Daru walked in silence for a while. "I didn't know any of that," he said finally.

"You didn't need to, until now," said Gabu. "Now it might be useful."

The Square

They arrived at the fountain at almost exactly the same time, from opposite sides of the square, which suggested that the reasoning on both sides had been similar, which Gabu had expected and which Suke-san, upon reflection, found either comforting or slightly unsettling, in proportions he hadn't yet finished working out.

The reunion was not dramatic. It was real, which is better. Dango crossed the square at speed and arrived at Daru in the way he always arrived at people he was glad to see — directly, completely, without the layers of management that most of his movements had. Daru received this in kind.

Suke-san stood in front of Gabu. He had thought about what to say during the walk to the square, which was unusual for him — he generally relied on the moment to provide the words. What the thinking had produced was: the truth, plainly stated, without the softening that would have felt dishonest.

"My approach didn't work. I tried to retrace steps I hadn't tracked carefully enough, and I couldn't do it. Dango identified a reliable local source and obtained the right information. We both contributed to getting here, but his contribution was more decisive than mine."
Gabu listened to all of this. Then: "You know what worked and what didn't. You're telling me accurately. That's more than most people do after being lost."
"I'm telling you because I want to understand it, not because it's comfortable to say."
"I know," Gabu said. "That's why it's worth something."

The fountain was as Gabu had remembered it: old stone, the water falling in a thin arc that caught the afternoon light in precisely the way it always had. Time had not changed it, which was neither a surprise nor entirely expected — you never know, with remembered things, whether the memory has improved them or faithfully kept them. This one had been faithfully kept.

"Is it the same?" Daru asked.
"The same," Gabu said. "Which is the best thing I know how to hope for, when I revisit something."

They walked home in a single group this time — no going ahead, no coming back — and the town contracted around them as familiar landmarks reappeared: the bakery, the corner with the florist, the long straight road that led back to the street that led back to the garden's gate.

Suke-san walked beside Dango. He had been thinking since the square, with the particular focus he brought to problems he hadn't finished solving.

"You said the inference and the assumption were the same thing," he said. "But I trust one more than the other."
"Yes," said Dango.
"What you actually did — looking at how the cat was sitting, inferring from her posture that she was a long-term resident — that's what I would call inference. What I was dismissing it as was assumption. The word I used changed what I did with the information."
"Okay," said Dango, who was following this but wasn't entirely sure where it was going.
"I think I use 'inference' for things I've generated through formal reasoning, and 'assumption' for things I've observed directly without formal structure. But direct observation is often more reliable than formal reasoning from incomplete premises. I've been systematically discounting the better information."

Dango thought about this for a moment. "You just described what I do," he said, "in a much longer way."

Suke-san considered. "Yes," he said. "I think so." He was quiet for a few steps. "That's also useful information."

The garden gate was ahead. The garden would be behind it: the stone, the porch, the old trees, the wall with its door that they now both knew about, all of it unchanged and present and, having been away from it, more clearly itself than it had been this morning.

Dango pushed the gate open. Behind him, Suke-san said nothing, which was the sound he made when something had settled into a place it was going to stay.

They were lost for a little while.
And then they found each other. 🗺️✨
