The Last Warm Afternoon

The first sign was the eating. Not once, but every day — Gabu and Daru moving methodically through the garden, working through the remaining greens with quiet focus, then settling in the last warm patches of afternoon sun. Their movements, already slow by nature, had taken on a different quality: deliberate, economical, as if each action were being saved for later.

Dango noticed. He noticed everything — that was both his gift and the thing that kept him up at night. He watched Gabu and Daru for several days before he found the right moment, and then he simply asked: "What are you getting ready for?"

Gabu finished chewing before he answered. "Come sit with us," he said. "All four of you. There is something I should say."

Something Is Different

They gathered by the blue stone as the late sun cast long shadows across the fading garden. Dango sat very close to Gabu. Suke-san positioned himself slightly behind — not outside the group, but at its edge, which was his way.

"Every winter, tortoises hibernate," Gabu said. "Our bodies require deep sleep in the cold months. It isn't something we choose — it is simply what we are. Daru and I will go inside our home, and we won't come out until spring. About four months."

Dango's whole posture changed. "Every year?"

"Every year."

"But I never — in all the time I've been here — I didn't know—"

"You arrived in spring," Daru said gently. "We had just woken up. You didn't know us yet when it happened before."

There was a particular quality to Dango's silences. They were wide, and many things happened inside them. This one lasted a long time.

"Will you still know us?" he asked at last. "When you wake up in spring — will you actually know us? Not just recognise us, but really know us?"
"Yes," Gabu said. "Some things don't sleep, even when the body does."

Suke-san had been completely still. He turned his head slightly, studying Gabu's face, then Dango's — as if reading something that needed careful reading.

"Promise," Dango said. Not quite a question.
"I promise," Gabu said. "Spring always comes. And I always come with it."

Dango believed him. He did. But believing a thing doesn't make the waiting feel shorter, and four months is a very long time when you spend every day with someone.

The Last Days

They had about three weeks. The weather shifted gradually — colder mornings, shorter afternoons, the birds beginning to thin out, the garden growing quiet in the way of things that are preparing for a long rest.

Dango spent every afternoon at the porch. He tried not to be too much — not to ask too many questions, not to crowd the space — but questions came out anyway. What did Gabu dream about? Did it feel peaceful or frightening? Would he be aware of time passing, or would it simply be gone?

Gabu answered each question simply and truthfully, without adding more comfort than the truth actually provided. Dango appreciated this, even when the answers were not particularly comforting.

Suke-san came most afternoons. He didn't ask questions. He just sat.

One afternoon, he found Gabu alone in the last warm patch of sun, and they sat together quietly for a while before Suke-san asked: "The ones who sleep — is it easier for them than for the ones who wait?"

"Yes," Gabu said. "The ones who wait always feel it more. But they're also the ones who feel the most joy when it ends."
"That's a very reasonable trade," Suke-san said, "for the one who comes back."
"Yes," Gabu said. "I suppose it is."

Suke-san thought about this. Something about the answer bothered him a little, and something about it helped a little, and he decided that was probably the most Gabu could do, and it was enough.

The Last Warm Afternoon

Tuesday arrived soft and warm, the sun at a low autumn angle that gilded everything and made it look like something from a long time ago. No one announced it. They gathered by the blue stone at midday and stayed there for most of the afternoon, not hurrying, not marking things as final, just being in the ordinary way — except that every ordinary thing had a different weight that day.

Daru talked. He talked about the summer in detail: the bee who had made a home near the tall flowers, the fat bird in the tree who had discovered that Dango would share his breakfast if asked nicely, an argument between two sparrows that had lasted three days. They laughed. They listened. It was good.

Then Gabu stood.

"Will you carry all of this with you?" Dango asked. "When you're asleep — will this afternoon be in there somewhere?"
"Yes," Gabu said. "All of it."

Dango pressed himself gently against Gabu's side and stayed there for a moment, his eyes closed. Then he stepped back.

"I'll take care of the stone," he said.
"I know," said Gabu. "I know you will."

Daru paused in the doorway and looked at all of them — at the garden, at Dango, at Suke-san, at the blue stone in the low golden light. "Don't let it get too quiet," he said. "We'll want to hear everything in spring."

Then the door was still.

Dango sat by the stone and didn't move. Suke-san sat beside him, close enough that their shoulders were nearly touching. The sky shifted through its colours slowly — gold, pink, the deep quiet blue of early evening — and neither of them spoke, and it was exactly the right kind of silence.

Spring

They came every morning through the winter. When snow fell on the stone, they cleared it without discussing it. Once, after a particularly heavy fall, Suke-san arrived before Dango and had already finished by the time Dango got there.

"You didn't have to," Dango said.
"I know," said Suke-san.

Spring arrived on a Wednesday, the way spring always arrives — not all at once but in a smell, a particular quality of light, a feeling in the air that something has shifted in the world's mind. Dango was at the stone, which is where he was every morning. He had been coming every morning for four months, and the habit of it had become its own kind of faith.

The door opened.

Daru came out first, blinking and slow, recalibrating to light and motion after months of stillness. He looked at the sky, at the new green things, at the blue stone with its small chips of light. Then he looked at Dango and his face became completely itself again.

Then Gabu. Slow, steady, unmistakably Gabu.

Dango walked to him and pressed his forehead gently against the old shell. He held it there. His eyes were closed.

"I came back," Gabu said.
"I know," Dango said. His voice had something in it. "I know you did."

Later that morning, Suke-san walked home through the new spring garden and thought about what he had witnessed — not just the reunion, but all of it: the announcement, the last days, the winter vigil, the morning by the stone.

He had been afraid. He could admit that now, at least to himself. Afraid for Gabu, for Daru, for the gap in the garden their absence would leave. And he had been right — the gap was real, and it had been there all winter.

But Gabu had also been right about the joy. He hadn't expected that part. He hadn't known how much light could come from a door opening on an ordinary Wednesday morning.

He thought about that for a long time on the walk home.

His tail was still moving when he got there.

Winter passed. Spring came.
And they were all together again. 🌱✨
