Suke-san Does Not Need Anyone

The garden had a new neighbour.

He arrived on a warm spring morning — a Shiba Inu puppy with soft red-brown fur, a white chest, and a small curly tail. His ears were still a little too big for his head, and his paws were still a little too big for his legs. Everything about him looked like it needed a bit more time to catch up to itself.

His name was Suke-san.

From the moment he arrived, Suke-san walked the top of the garden wall with his chin high and his tail raised. He studied the flowers, the trees, and the shapes of the clouds moving across the sky. He did not look for anyone to talk to.

I am completely fine on my own, he thought. I don't need anyone.

A yellow butterfly drifted past his nose, and his eyes followed it without meaning to.

Just for a moment, his tail began to wag.

He made it stop immediately.
He looked around to see if anyone had noticed.
Good. No one had.

The First Morning

Dango had spotted the new puppy from the other side of the garden.

Dango wasn't a small puppy anymore. He had grown bigger and stronger, though his legs had stayed exactly as short as they had always been. Every morning, he walked a slow circuit of the whole garden — checking on the birds, the bees, the best patches of cool dirt, and anything unusual. He had decided, without anyone asking him, that watching over the garden was his job.

And today, he had noticed something new.

"A new friend!" Dango said, and ran over as fast as his short legs could carry him.

Suke-san saw the orange-and-white dog running towards him at full speed. He sat up very straight and arranged his face into an expression of calm dignity.

"Hello," said Suke-san, with a small, precise bow. "I am Suke-san."

"I'm Dango! Come with me — I want to introduce you to my friends! You're going to love them!"

Suke-san looked at him. Then he looked at the garden. He decided to follow, just to see what was there.

Near the old porch, two tortoises were sitting in the warm morning light. A small blue stone sat at the entrance to their home. One tortoise was examining a leaf with deep concentration. The other was stretching his neck as far as it would go, trying to reach a flower.

"Gabu! Daru! This is Suke-san! He just moved in!" Dango announced.

The tortoise who had been studying the leaf looked up slowly. He had large, calm eyes. "Hello," he said. "I am Gabu. You are welcome here."

The other tortoise spun around at once. "Hi! I'm Daru! Have you been on this street long? What do you enjoy eating? Have you noticed the fat bird in the big tree? He's perfectly round — like a ball that grew wings!"

Suke-san looked at them carefully. They were small, round, and moved at a pace that was almost too slow to call movement.

"Hello," he said. "You are tortoises."

"Yes!" said Daru.
"You move very slowly," said Suke-san.
"Yes," agreed Gabu.

Daru tilted his head and said: "Your ears are very big."

Suke-san stood up a little taller. "They are normal-sized ears," he said.

"They're very cute," said Daru.

Suke-san opened his mouth. He closed it. He looked somewhere else.

But something about the quiet one — Gabu — made him linger. Gabu had already gone back to his leaf, studying it with complete attention, as though nothing else existed. He wasn't trying to entertain anyone or make an impression. He was simply there, doing exactly what he wanted to do.

Suke-san understood that feeling very well.

"It was good to meet you," he said. He turned and walked back to the wall.

From the top of the wall, he sat and watched the garden below. The two tortoises. The short, energetic dog. The blue stone by the door, catching the morning light.

He didn't need any of that.

He watched them for a while anyway.

The Rainy Afternoon

Three days went by. Each morning, Suke-san walked the wall by himself. He watched the birds and the shifting clouds. He did not go near the porch.

On the fourth day, the sky turned dark and heavy.

Suke-san was walking the wall when the rain began — big, cold drops that fell without warning. He jumped down quickly and ran under a tree. The water came through the branches and hit him anyway.

He moved to a different tree. He was still getting wet.

Then the thunder arrived.

A long, deep BOOM that seemed to shake the whole sky.

Suke-san's legs went stiff. His ears pressed flat against his head. He looked around very quickly in all directions.

He was fine. He was absolutely fine. He was not scared at all. Not even a little.

Another BOOM.

Suke-san ran.

He wasn't sure where he was going. His big paws splashed through the soaking grass. He ran past the tall flowers, past the old oak tree — and then he stopped.

He was standing in front of the porch.

The small blue stone sat by the door. Even in the grey, dim rain, it was catching the light.

Daru's head appeared in the doorway. "Suke-san! Come in — it's dry in here!"

Suke-san looked at the small, low space. He looked back at the cold, dark garden. He walked in and sat near the entrance.

He breathed slowly and focused on being completely calm. He was fine. There was nothing to worry about.

"Was that scary?" Daru asked.
"No," said Suke-san.

"Oh," said Daru. "My legs start shaking a little when the thunder gets very loud. Even now, sometimes."

Suke-san looked at him. "Yours too?" he said.

"Yes. But it always passes," said Daru.

Outside, Dango was running through the rain in large, joyful circles, barking at the sky as if he were having the best time of his life. His whole round body was completely soaked. He looked absolutely thrilled.

"He isn't scared?" Suke-san asked.
"Dango is only frightened of one thing," said Daru.
"What is it?"
"The vacuum cleaner."

Suke-san watched Dango leap into a deep puddle with both front paws. Something moved inside his chest. A small sound escaped him — short and involuntary.

Almost a laugh.
He stopped it immediately.

Gabu had been sitting quietly the whole time, watching the rain with his usual steady expression. Then, without turning his head, he said: "The blue stone — would you like to hear the story of how it came to be here?"

"...Yes," said Suke-san.

So Daru told the story from the beginning. How Gabu had found the stone near the water bowl and carried it home. How he had fixed it to his shell with tree sap and worn it for nearly a month. How Dango had accidentally buried it while digging, and then found it again with his nose in the fading evening light. And how Gabu had decided that it shouldn't belong to only one of them.

"He said: let's put it at the door, so all of us can see it every day," Daru finished.

Suke-san looked at the stone for a long time. "You three have built something together," he said quietly.

"Yes," said Daru. He smiled. "You could be part of it too, if you wanted."

Suke-san didn't answer. But he didn't move, either.

The rain continued for a long time. Daru talked about the fat bird, a red berry he had found that week, and a very interesting pebble near the water bowl. Gabu added a quiet comment now and then. Suke-san mostly listened. He didn't say much, but he didn't leave — and as the time passed, sitting there in the dry warmth while the rain fell outside, he noticed that it had stopped feeling strange.

When the rain finally stopped, he stood up and shook the dampness from his fur. "Thank you," he said. "For the shelter."

"You're welcome here whenever you like," said Daru.

Suke-san stepped outside and paused for a moment, looking down at the blue stone.

Then he walked out into the clean, rain-washed garden.

Behind him, without thinking about it, his small curly tail moved once.

The Morning He Came Back

The next morning was bright and clear, with the kind of freshness that only comes after heavy rain.

Daru was the first one up, as he always was. He walked out into the cool morning air and stretched his legs slowly, enjoying the way the flowers smelled after a good wash. A small bird somewhere in the big tree was singing the same four notes over and over.

Daru stopped.

Suke-san was sitting near the porch. He hadn't knocked or called out. He was just sitting there very quietly, with his large ears up and his eyes moving slowly across the garden.

"Good morning," said Daru.
"Good morning," said Suke-san. "I was walking near here. I came this way."

Daru sat down next to him without saying anything more. Together they watched the morning light move slowly across the grass, turning it from silver to gold.

"It's beautiful," Suke-san said, almost to himself.
"Yes," said Daru. "Every morning is a little bit different."

Gabu came out of the porch, one step at a time. He saw Suke-san, and gave him a slow nod — the same nod as always. Then he walked to his favourite warm spot and settled into it.

Then Dango came racing across the garden from the far side. The moment he saw Suke-san, his whole body became one enormous expression of happiness.

"You came back! I told them you would come back! I knew it!"

Suke-san sat up straight. "I was just passing—"

But Dango was already bouncing around him, and his big ears were flapping with each jump, and before Suke-san could gather himself —

— his tail began to wag.

He stopped it quickly. But not quickly enough.

"Your tail!" Daru said, smiling broadly.
"It was the wind," said Suke-san.
"There isn't any wind today," said Daru.

Suke-san studied the sky. He examined a flower near his paw. He found a very interesting spot on the ground and looked at it carefully.

"...Perhaps a small wind," he said.

Dango sat down in front of him and looked up with shining eyes. "Will you stay for a while? We mostly just sit here in the morning. Sometimes a rabbit comes by. One time there was a frog — this big—" He opened his jaws as wide as they would go.

Suke-san was quiet.

He looked at Gabu, still and slow in the warm sunshine, needing nothing from anyone. He looked at Daru, who had already started trying to sniff the flower next to him. He looked at Dango, who had completely forgotten about the frog and was now watching a bee with total concentration.

They were the strangest collection of creatures he had ever seen.

"I'm not very good at sitting still," Suke-san said.
"That's all right," said Daru. "Gabu isn't very good at going fast. I'm not very good at being quiet. And Dango—"
"Hey!" said Dango.
"—has his own particular challenges," Daru finished kindly.

Suke-san sat down beside Daru.

"I'll stay," he said. "For a while."

The morning sun continued its slow crossing of the sky. The fat yellow butterfly came by and floated past all of them. Dango's tail moved lazily back and forth on the warm ground. Gabu didn't move, but the expression around his old, patient eyes was soft and open.

Nobody spoke for a long time.

Suke-san sat very still and tried to look very calm.

But underneath his white chest, something warm had been quietly growing for a while now. He still didn't have the right word for it. He thought he would wait and see if it kept growing.

He had a feeling it would.

That evening, Suke-san walked home at his usual unhurried pace.

He thought about the blue stone and how it had ended up at the door. He thought about the rain, and the sound of Daru's voice telling a story in the dark. He thought about how Dango was only afraid of one thing, and how Gabu's nod had said you are welcome here without using a single word.

Suke-san did not need anyone.

He was a Shiba Inu. He was perfectly fine on his own. He was proud and independent and calm and he absolutely did not need —

A leaf tumbled past him in the evening air.

His eyes followed it all the way to the ground.

His tail wagged.

He looked around. No one had seen.

He walked home with his chin up and his tail high.
But this time, his thoughts were already on tomorrow morning.

The garden had a new neighbour.
And now — he was not alone. 🐾✨
