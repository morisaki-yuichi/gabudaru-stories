The Giant in the Garden
and the Missing Stone

Spring rearranges things. The garden that had been one thing in the cold months became, quietly and without announcement, something else: louder, more colourful, crowded with its own abundance. Gabu and Daru had grown into it, and it had grown around them, and their friendship had settled into the particular ease of two things that have found, over time, exactly how to fit together.

The arrival of Dango did not fit into anything. Dango arrived the way spring itself arrives — all at once, everywhere, impossible to ignore, and somehow making the garden feel like it had been quietly incomplete before.

The Arrival of Dango

The ground shook first. Then the sound arrived — a full-throated, ecstatic barking that contained within it the entire enthusiasm of a creature who has just discovered that the world is enormous and interesting and that he is apparently in it.

"Woof! Woof! Thump, thump, thump!"

He was a Corgi — a Corgi puppy, to be precise, though the word puppy didn't quite capture the scope of him. He had a long body, a propeller for a tail, ears that were still in the middle of deciding their final form, and a quality of presence that seemed to exceed his actual physical size by a considerable margin. He ran three complete circles around Gabu, who had not yet fully processed that something was running circles around him, then came to a stop six inches from Daru's face.

"Hello! Hello! What are you? I've never seen anything like you. Are you very old? You look quite old. Are you fast? Can you dig? I can dig. Do you want to see me dig?"

"One question at a time," Gabu said, with infinite patience.

"I'm Daru! What's your name? Can you sit still for even one second? Your legs are so short!"

"I'm Dango! I can absolutely sit still! Watch—"

He sat still for approximately one second and then stood up again, apparently satisfied by the demonstration.

Despite — or possibly because of — the chaos he introduced into the garden's daily rhythms, Dango proved impossible not to love. He was noise and motion and the constant warm interruption of an ordinary afternoon, and Gabu and Daru, who had been two for a very long time, found themselves unexpectedly three.

A Terrible Loss

The stone had been part of Gabu for so long that its loss, when it came, felt disproportionate to what it was. It was a small thing — smooth, blue, unremarkable to anyone who didn't know its history. He had found it weeks before beside the water bowl, attracted by the depth of its colour, and had fixed it to the centre of his shell with tree sap. It was not a grand gesture. It was simply something he had done, quietly and for no one's benefit but his own.

It was gone when Daru looked at it in the cooling light of late afternoon, while Gabu slept nearby in the heat-exhausted quiet. Just an absence — a pale oval on the dark shell, where something had been and was no longer.

"Gabu," Daru said carefully, when his friend opened his eyes. "I need to tell you something, and I want you to take it calmly."

"I'm always calm," Gabu said.

"Your stone," Daru said. "It isn't there."

Gabu was calm. He was extremely calm in the specific way of someone who is experiencing a feeling they have decided not to express in full. He turned his head as far as it would go to see the back of his own shell, which was not far enough. "I see," he said.

Dango, who had woken at the sound of voices and come bounding over, read the room with surprising accuracy and went immediately and impressively still. "What's happened?" he asked quietly.

The Search Party

They searched with the thoroughness of those who understood what they were looking for — not a stone, exactly, but the thing the stone represented: a small, specific piece of someone's sense of themselves, which had been worn without ceremony and would be missed without making a fuss about it.

Dango worked with his nose. The blue stone had a smell — mineral, faintly cold, with something underneath that was distinctly Gabu — and he followed the traces of it through the grass in slow, overlapping arcs. Gabu and Daru moved in careful parallels. They covered the area twice.

Nothing.

It was the hole that told the story. Dango's hole, deep and fresh from that afternoon's enthusiasm, sitting in the earth exactly where Gabu had been sleeping.

"Dango," Daru said, as gently as he could.

Dango was already at the hole. His ears were flat. His tail was completely still. "I didn't mean to," he said, very quietly. "I would never have — I didn't know it was there, I was just digging, I—"

"Dango," Gabu said. "Find it."

Something in Gabu's voice — not harsh, but very direct, trusting in a way that was almost harder to bear than anger — moved Dango entirely. He lowered his nose to the soil of his own excavation and began again, moving with a delicacy that seemed physically at odds with everything else about him, separating the earth in thin layers like pages of a book.

He found it seven minutes later. It was the smell that led him — cold and mineral and Gabu — and he cleared the last centimetre of soil from around it with the side of his nose and left it there in the last of the light: small, blue, intact.

True Friendship

Daru carried it to Gabu without speaking. Gabu held it for a long moment. The sap was gone — dissolved entirely by the earth — and the stone would not adhere again without a new application, and looking at the pale circle it had left on his shell, something in Gabu's expression shifted. Not grief, exactly. Something more like recognition.

"Dango," he said. "You found it."

"I buried it," Dango said.

"Yes. And then you found it, in the dark, in soil you'd moved yourself, with nothing to guide you except your nose." He paused. "I have never been able to find anything with my nose. I find that genuinely impressive."

Dango's tail moved — tentatively, as though unsure it was allowed to.

Gabu looked at the stone, then at Daru, then at the entrance to their home under the porch. A small, clean threshold. The blue stone, held in his mouth, catching the last of the light.

"I wore this because I liked looking at it," he said. "But I think it means more here." He set it at the door with care. "At the entrance. Where it belongs to all of us."

Daru didn't say anything for a moment. Then: "You know what that is, don't you? That's exactly what you are."

"What do you mean?"

"Something small and blue and worth looking at every day."

Gabu turned his head away in the dignified manner of someone who has been paid a compliment and doesn't know what to do with it.

Dango's tail was at full speed.

That evening, the stone sat at the door and caught the moonlight,
and the garden had found its third. 🐾✨
