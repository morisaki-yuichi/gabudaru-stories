The Long Way Round

The market visit had been Gabu's idea, which surprised everyone in different ways — Dango because Gabu rarely suggested outings, Suke-san because he hadn't anticipated that Gabu had places he wanted to revisit, and Daru not at all, because Daru believed Gabu capable of anything and found all of his choices equally inevitable.

"There is a fountain in the main square," Gabu had said. "I saw it once, a long time ago. I'd like to see it again." And from that single sentence, somehow, the outing had been arranged and the morning had arrived.

The town was the kind of busy that the garden was not: layered and fast, with competing sounds and smells and people moving in all directions at once, each of them with their own complete purpose. Dango tried to track all of it and managed to track none of it very well. Suke-san walked with his usual composure, but his eyes moved more than usual.

The natural pace differential created a loose configuration: Dango and Suke-san moving ahead, pausing, coming back, then moving ahead again — the way a current branches around a slower-moving centre. It had worked fine in the garden. The garden didn't have delivery trucks.

The truck came through the narrow market street with its horn going and the crowd on the pavement pressing back, and when the movement resolved and the crowd resettled, Gabu and Daru were simply gone — not visibly gone, but not visibly there, which in a busy street amounts to the same thing.

Thread One: The Lost

Suke-san's first instinct was retracing, which was the logical choice in principle. In practice, it required better knowledge of the route than either of them had accumulated, because they had been moving forward without systematically noting landmarks, which is the difference between travel and navigation.

After two wrong turns and one street that looked correct from one end and wrong from the other, Suke-san stopped. He stood in the middle of the pavement — which required a conscious effort not to move while people flowed past — and ran through what he actually knew rather than what he was extrapolating from assumption. The result was less than he had expected.

Dango sat down beside a shop door. On the window ledge above: a large grey cat, watching the street with the unhurried interest of a long-term resident.

"Excuse me," Dango said. "Do you know where the big stone fountain is? In the market square."
"Two streets south," said the cat. "Left at the bakery. You'll hear it before you see it."
"Thank you very much."

Suke-san looked at the cat. He looked at Dango. Something in his expression was working through the fact that this had worked, which implied something about his own approach not having worked, which implied something about the assumption underlying that approach.

"You just... asked," he said.
"It was the obvious thing," Dango said. "She's been here a long time. She knows the streets."
"You don't know how long she's been here."
"I made a reasonable inference," Dango said, in the careful tone of someone using a phrase they had recently learned.

Suke-san looked at him. A reasonable inference. "Which way is south?" he asked.

Dango looked at the position of the sun, the shadows on the buildings. "That way," he said.

Two streets in that direction, left at the bakery — they heard the fountain before they saw it: a continuous soft sound under the market noise, cleaner than the street sounds, coming from an open square.

They stood at the edge of the square and looked at the fountain.

"I wouldn't have asked the cat," Suke-san said.
"I know," said Dango. "I would have, eventually."
"I might not have. I would have kept trying to work it out from first principles."
Dango thought about this. "What's first principles?"
"Working only from what you know for certain."
"Oh," said Dango. "I usually just ask."

Thread Two: The Waiting

Gabu had not panicked. This was not because he was without concern — he was, in fact, moderately concerned — but because panic and concern occupy the same body differently, and he had found, over a long life, that the former rarely improved on the latter as a way of addressing a situation.

He found a spot out of the flow of foot traffic and stood there thinking while Daru scanned the crowd for familiar shapes.

"We should look for them," Daru said. He was keeping his voice steady with visible effort.
"We'd miss each other," Gabu said. "Moving targets rarely find each other by both moving. One party needs to go to a fixed point. The right fixed point."
"The fountain," Daru said immediately.
"Why?"
"Because Suke-san will think of a meeting point, and the fountain is the most logical one. It's central, it's visible, it's the thing we came to see."
Gabu looked at him. "Yes," he said. "Exactly."

Daru's anxiety did not disappear — it was still there, clearly, in the way he kept looking around — but it reorganised itself slightly around the presence of a plan. They began making their way through the market toward the square.

"How can you be this calm?" Daru asked, after a while.
"I'm not certain I am calm," Gabu said. "I'm certain that being visibly not-calm wouldn't help. I've been lost before — genuinely lost, in a much larger and more confusing place than this. I learned something from it."
"What?"
"That fear and clear thinking are not the same switch. You can have both at once. The fear is there, but the thinking still works — it just takes more deliberate effort. I've had more practice than you at making the effort."

They walked in silence for a moment.

"Where were you lost?" Daru asked.
"This was before I found the garden. I was traveling — not going anywhere in particular, which is both the best and worst state to be lost in. Best because there's no deadline. Worst because there's no obvious destination to orient toward."
"Were you alone?"
"Completely."
"For how long?"
"Three days. I found my way by following water downstream. Water always goes somewhere eventually."

Daru thought about this for several streets. The image of Gabu — young, alone, lost for three days, following a stream — sat oddly against his understanding of Gabu as someone who had always simply been where he was, settled and permanent. "You were really afraid," he said.

"Yes," Gabu said. "I was really afraid. But I found the way." He paused. "Dango and Suke-san will too."

The Square

Both groups arrived at the fountain within minutes of each other, from opposite sides of the square. Dango saw them first — spotted Daru's shape at the far edge — and covered the distance in a way that confirmed his legs worked fine.

What followed was not dramatic. It was better than dramatic: it was genuine. Daru and Dango pressed together without ceremony. Gabu and Suke-san faced each other at a more measured distance.

"I couldn't navigate," Suke-san said. He said it directly, without softening. "My approach didn't work. Dango found help. We got here together, but the credit belongs to him more than to me."
"You identified the right meeting point," Dango said. "And you knew which direction south was."
"I know which direction south is," Suke-san said. "That's a very low bar."
"It was the right bar at the right time," Gabu said. He looked at Suke-san with the particular attention he reserved for things he wanted someone to actually hear. "You combined your knowledge with Dango's approach and it worked. That's not a failure. That's collaboration."

Suke-san considered this. He had a precise relationship with credit and blame — perhaps too precise — and he was aware that "collaboration" and "I failed and he saved us" were different framings of the same sequence of events. He was also aware that Gabu was right, which he found slightly irritating and mostly useful.

The fountain was exactly as Gabu had remembered it: old stone, water falling in a thin curtain into the basin, light catching it differently as the afternoon moved. It was, as he had said, beautiful. Less surprising than it had been the first time, but no less beautiful for that.

They walked home together — all four at the compromised pace that everyone's different speeds produced — and the town shrank behind them as familiar landmarks reappeared.

Suke-san walked beside Dango, not ahead. "I want to ask you something," he said. "When you asked the cat — did you hesitate?"

"No," Dango said. "Was I supposed to?"
"I would have," Suke-san said. "I would have thought: is this a reliable source, is this the most efficient approach, is there a better option I haven't considered. I would have spent several minutes not asking before I asked."
"Oh," Dango said. He thought about this. "I don't think about whether it's the best option. I just think: who might know? And then I ask them."
"That's faster."
"It misses things, though. You would have thought about whether the cat was trustworthy."
"Was she?"
"Yes," Dango said. "But that was luck."
"No," said Suke-san. "That was a reasonable inference based on observable evidence. Which I think you made without realising it." He paused. "You just call it asking."

Dango thought about this all the way home and wasn't sure what to make of it, but it felt important in a way he wanted to keep.

They were lost for a little while.
And then they found each other. 🗺️✨
