When Dango Was Still

Every morning, Dango made a full circuit of the garden before breakfast. He checked the tall flowers by the fence, the shady ground under the old tree, the corner where the rabbit sometimes came, the porch where the blue stone sat in the morning light. He knew every part of the garden well. It was his to look after, and he was good at it.

One morning in early summer, he was doing his usual rounds near the old fence when his right front paw caught on a root he hadn't noticed. He stumbled. He lifted his paw. It hurt — not terribly, but enough.

Gabu came over and looked at it carefully. "Rest for two or three days," he said. "Don't run on it."

Dango looked at the garden. The corners unvisited. The things unchecked. "Two or three days?" he said.

"Yes," said Gabu simply.

Dango sat down. He did not argue. But this, he could already tell, was going to be very difficult.

The First Day

He settled by the blue stone and tried to be comfortable with sitting still.

He was not comfortable at first. There was too much to do. Too many corners. Too many flowers to smell, birds to track, patches of earth to check. Being in one spot felt like wearing a coat that didn't fit — itchy and wrong and too tight everywhere.

But after a while, something shifted.

A small black beetle was crossing the ground near him. He had seen this beetle many times before — it lived somewhere near the porch — but he had never had time to follow it with his eyes. Today, he did. The beetle walked up to the blue stone, went underneath it, and came out the other side. Then it turned and did the same thing again.

It always goes under the stone, Dango thought. Every single time. I never knew that.

Daru came and settled next to him in the afternoon. He told stories — things from before Dango had arrived, things that had happened in the garden years ago. Dango listened to every word. He had heard some of these before, but not like this, not all the way through without moving away to check on something else.

Gabu sat on his other side, reading the warm afternoon with his usual quiet patience. Just there. It was enough.

The Second Day

Suke-san came early in the morning and sat down near Dango without making a thing of it.

"You came before Daru," Dango noticed.
"Yes," Suke-san said. He didn't explain further. They sat together in the morning light, which was gentle and unhurried and full of birds.

At midday, Dango asked Gabu the question he had been thinking about since the day before: "When you sit still for a long time, what are you doing? You don't seem bored. Is it hard not to be bored?"

"I'm not trying not to be bored," Gabu said. "I'm watching. Listening. When I stop moving, the world comes closer — things I walk past and never notice. A cloud changing shape. The smell of something underground. Small sounds."

"Can you teach me to do that?"
"You can try it now," Gabu said. "Close your eyes. Be still for a little while. Just let things come."

Dango closed his eyes. He breathed slowly. He tried to be still — not sleeping, not restless, but simply present.

He heard: a bird in the very top of the old tree, a sound he had heard a thousand times before but never really listened to. The wind moving through the tall dry grass near the fence. The faint buzzing of a bee, somewhere past the flower beds.

He opened his eyes. "Those sounds were always there," he said.

"Yes," Gabu said. "You were always moving through them."

— ✦ —

The Third Day

On the third morning, Suke-san was already at the stone when Dango came out — earlier than Daru, earlier than Gabu, sitting in the fresh morning air with his ears up and his expression composed.

"You're here very early," Dango said.
Suke-san was quiet for a moment, as though deciding something. Then: "If it were me who couldn't move, you would simply be here. Without being asked. I've been thinking about that, and I wanted to try to be like that."

Dango looked at him. Suke-san was looking at the garden, not at Dango, his expression neutral and careful and — underneath it — something warm.

"Thank you," Dango said.

Suke-san gave one small nod.

That afternoon, Dango tried his paw carefully. It felt much better. He stood, took a few steps, then a few more. The paw held. He looked at Gabu.

"Tomorrow," Gabu said, "you can run."

Dango sat back down beside the stone for one last afternoon. He watched the beetle make its rounds. He listened to the birds. He let the afternoon be what it was.

— ✦ —

After

The next morning, Dango ran. His paw was completely fine, and the feeling of moving through the garden again — the wind, the familiar ground, the smells all arriving fast and one after another — was wonderful in the way that things are wonderful after you've been without them.

He ran past the old fence and the tall flowers and the big tree and the corner where the rabbit sometimes came. He was making good time. Everything was checked, everything was right.

And then, somewhere in the middle of the garden, he stopped.

Not because he was tired. Not because he had to. He just stopped, and sat down, right there in the middle of the open grass.

He listened.

The bird at the top of the old tree.
The wind in the dry grass near the fence.
Somewhere far away, a bee.

He sat there for a while, just listening. Then he stood up and ran on, happy and fast, all the way to the end of the garden and back.

That evening, he told Gabu what he had done — not the injury, not the three days, but the stopping in the middle of the circuit. The sitting on purpose.

Gabu listened without interrupting. When Dango finished, he said: "Good."

Just that.

Dango thought about it on the walk home. The garden was the same garden it had always been. The sounds were the same sounds. The morning light was the same morning light.

But he was different in it now. Just slightly. Just enough.

The garden was the same.
But now Dango knew how to listen to it. 🌿✨
