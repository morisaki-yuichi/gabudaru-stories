The Door in the Wall

Dango had walked the garden's perimeter thousands of times. He knew every stone, every patch of shadow, every place the grass grew differently. He had never — not once — noticed the door.

It was at the very far end of the garden, behind the oldest tree, half-buried under decades of ivy that had grown over it so completely that the door had become part of the wall, which had become part of the garden, which had become part of what Dango understood as simply: everything that was here.

On a clear morning in early spring, a loose tendril of ivy had fallen away overnight, and there — underneath — was the edge of a wooden door, and iron hinges, and a latch.

He pushed it. It swung open on surprisingly quiet hinges. And beyond it: a narrow path, and then open space, and the smell of water somewhere far away.

He ran back at full speed.

"There is a door in the wall," he said, barely stopping. "At the far end. I never knew. Come and see."

Gabu lifted his head from the patch of morning sun he had been occupying. "I know that door," he said.

This stopped Dango entirely. "You know it."

"Yes."
"For how long?"
"Always."
"Why didn't you ever—"
"You never asked," Gabu said. He said it without apology or evasion, as a simple statement of how things had been.

Daru was already moving toward the far end of the garden. "Are we going through? We are going through. Come on."

Gabu considered for a moment. Then he stood, slowly, in the unhurried way he did everything. "Yes," he said. "I think it is time."

The Door

They stood at the door in a line. Up close, it was beautiful in a ruined way — the wood weathered silver-grey, the iron latch worn smooth by hands that had touched it long ago.

Dango went first, then Suke-san, then Daru, then Gabu last — who paused in the doorway for just a moment, his old eyes moving over the garden behind them before he stepped through.

The narrow passage opened into a wide meadow. The sky was enormous. There were no walls in any direction — just grass rolling away in long waves, a stand of old trees to the east, and somewhere ahead, the sound of water.

The air smelled different: a cooler, wilder version of the garden's familiar scents, with something earthy underneath that the garden never quite had.

Dango looked around at all of it for approximately three seconds. Then he ran.

He ran through the long grass, which brushed his ears as he went. He ran past yellow wildflowers and through a drift of white ones and over a small rise and — stopped.

The path divided. Two tracks going in different directions, both disappearing into more grass. He stood at the fork with his usual certainty completely absent, which was a new and interesting experience.

He had run ahead.
He had expected to know what to do when he got there.
He had not known what to do when he got there.
He waited.

Into the Open

When the others reached him, Suke-san assessed the fork methodically: the angle of the sun, the direction of the light breeze, the way the grass was slightly more worn on one path than the other.

"I can hear water," he said carefully. "It's faint, but it's there, and it's coming from the right. Based on the wind direction, the river should be—" He paused. "Right," he concluded. "We go right."

Gabu said: "Right is correct." Just that, no elaboration.

Suke-san nodded, as though he had been certain all along. Dango noticed the slight delay before the nod and said nothing about it. Some things were better left as they were.

The right-hand path led through stands of old trees — taller and denser than the garden's, with roots that emerged from the earth and curved back in like the backs of sleeping animals. The light through the canopy was different from the garden's light: moving, broken, appearing and disappearing in patches.

The sounds were different too. More birds, and different ones — calls Dango didn't recognise, coming from places he couldn't see. Rustling in the undergrowth that paused as they passed and started again after.

Dango found himself staying closer to the group than he usually would. Not from fear, exactly. Something more like respect for not-knowing.

And then, clearly: the sound of moving water.

The River

The river was wide and fast and very bright, catching the afternoon sun in pieces. It moved over smooth round stones with a continuous sound that was somehow both busy and peaceful. Small silver fish darted in the shallows. A heron stood motionless in the current with the absolute patience of something that has learned to wait.

Daru made a sound that was not really a word, walked to the bank, and sat down facing the water. He stayed completely still, which was remarkable for him, and just looked.

Gabu walked to the water's edge. He stood there for a long time without speaking, looking at the far bank and the trees beyond it.

"This is why you agreed to come," Suke-san said. It wasn't a question.
"Yes," Gabu said. "I wanted to see if it was the same."
"Is it?"
"Mostly," Gabu said. "The river is the same. The heron may be a different heron. The stones are rearranged by every flood. But it is the same river."

"You came here to decide something," Suke-san said. He said it carefully, not pressing.
"Yes. A long time ago, I was trying to decide whether to stay in the garden. I came here to think."
"And you decided to stay."
"I decided to go back first," Gabu said. "I went back, and then I stayed. Those are different choices."

Dango waded in to his belly, gasped at the cold, and didn't go any deeper. Daru found a small pebble — grey, smooth, with one white line through it like a seam of light — and turned it over and over in his hands.

"The river's," Gabu said, before Daru could ask.
"I know," Daru said. He set it on a flat stone above the waterline, where it could be found, or could stay.

They stayed until the light started to change.

The Long Way Back

Paths looked different going the other way. This was something Dango had never had occasion to discover in the garden, where every path was memorised from both directions. Out here, familiar landmarks turned unfamiliar when seen from behind, and more than once he felt the small cold nudge of not-quite-knowing-where-he-was.

He did not run ahead.

At the fork, he stopped and thought — actually thought, not just ran and hoped. The yellow flowers had been on his right going out. Coming back, they should be on his left.

"Left," he said.
Suke-san looked at him. "How do you know?"
"The flowers," Dango said. "They were on my right going out."

Suke-san studied the flowers. Then he nodded — not his usual immediate nod, but a considered one. "Yes. That's right."

They went left. It was right.

The narrow passage, the door, and on the other side: the garden, exactly as it had been, waiting quietly.

Dango sat by the blue stone and looked at the garden. It was the same garden. Nothing had changed while they were away.

But his relationship to it had changed slightly, which is its own kind of difference.

Suke-san sat beside him. "I wasn't certain at the fork," he said, after a while. "The first time. I made a reasonable inference. It could have been wrong."

"It wasn't," Dango said.

"This time." A pause. "Next time I might be wrong. It's useful to know that."

Daru placed a second stone beside the blue one — small, brownish-grey, from the path near the trees. Two stones. One found, one brought back.

Gabu sat in his usual spot in the evening sun. He looked at the garden with the expression he usually had — patient, settled, as if everything were exactly as it should be. But Dango thought, watching him, that there was something else in it now. Something that had been there all along, but that he only understood now, having seen where it came from.

The garden was the same garden.
But they had chosen to come back to it. 🌿✨
