When Dango Was Still

Dango's morning circuit was a point of personal pride. He had been doing it every day since he was old enough to know the garden well: a full loop from the porch, past the old fence, under the big tree, around the flower beds, checking the corner where the rabbit sometimes appeared, back past the water bowl, and home again. He knew which stones had been moved overnight, which flowers were opening, which birds had arrived and which had gone. It was his way of being in the world, and he was good at it.

On a Tuesday morning in early summer, he was running the familiar path near the old fence when his right front paw found a root that hadn't been there yesterday — or rather, had been there all along, but he had never caught it before. He stumbled, stopped, and lifted the paw to look at it.

It hurt. Not badly, but clearly.

Gabu examined it and said what Dango was already afraid he would say: "Rest it. Two or three days. No running."

Dango looked at the garden with the expression of someone who has been handed a task they are not sure they are built for.

"Two or three days," he repeated.
"Yes," said Gabu, already walking away.

The First Day

Dango settled by the blue stone and tried to make the best of it.

The first hour was the hardest. There was so much to do — corners that needed checking, things that needed knowing — and not being able to do it made him restless in a way that felt almost physical, like an itch he couldn't scratch. His eyes kept moving to the edges of the garden, to the places where something might be happening without him.

But eventually, something unexpected happened: the garden came to him.

A small black beetle — one he had seen countless times in passing — crossed the ground in front of him and went under the blue stone. He watched it go. Then it came back out the other side. Then it turned and went under again. He watched it do this four times.

It always does that, he realised. It always goes under the stone and comes back. Every single day. I never knew, because I was always moving.

Daru came in the afternoon and sat with him, telling stories from the garden's longer history — things that had happened before Dango arrived, before Suke-san, seasons and visitors and small dramas that had left no visible mark but were real nonetheless. Dango listened without interrupting, which was unusual for him. The stories turned out to be very good.

Gabu sat on his other side without speaking, reading the afternoon in his particular way. The three of them were there together in the warm still air, and it was not nothing. It was, in fact, quite a lot.

The Second Day

Suke-san came early and sat down without making any announcement about it. They sat in the fresh morning light together, watching the garden wake up — the birds coming and going, the flowers opening, the first bee of the day following its own private logic from flower to flower.

At midday, Dango asked the question he had been forming since the day before: "Gabu — when you sit still for hours, what's happening? You don't seem bored. You don't seem to be waiting for something. What is it that you're doing?"

"Attending," Gabu said.
"Attending?"
"Giving my attention to what's actually here. Boredom is what happens when you're present but your mind is somewhere else — wishing it were different, waiting for it to change. Attending is when you're fully where you are. They feel completely different."

Dango considered this. "Can I try?"

"You've been trying for a day and a half," Gabu said. "You're doing it already, in parts."

Dango closed his eyes and tried to be more deliberate about it. He let himself be where he was — by the stone, in the afternoon warmth, with the sound of the garden around him. He listened without looking for anything in particular.

A thrush in the top branches of the old tree — not just the sound of a bird, but this bird, this particular call, going up at the end like a question.
The wind coming through the tall dry grass near the fence, which rustled in its own rhythm, separate from everything else.
Far away, the low hum of bees working the late flowers.

"I've walked past all of that every day," Dango said slowly, opening his eyes. "I never heard any of it."

"Motion is a kind of noise," Gabu said. "When you stop, things get quieter."

The Third Day

On the third morning, Suke-san was already there when Dango came out. The garden was still pale and cool, the dew still on the grass, the sun just clearing the far fence. Suke-san was sitting upright by the stone with his ears forward, watching something in the middle distance that turned out, on closer inspection, to be nothing in particular.

"You're very early," Dango said.
Suke-san was quiet for a moment. When he spoke, he looked at the garden rather than at Dango. "I've been thinking about how you are when someone you care about is in trouble. You don't make a big thing of it. You don't announce it. You're simply there — you just appear, as if it's the obvious thing, which I suppose for you it is." He paused. "I wanted to try to be that way. To simply be here, without it being a favour or a performance."
"Is it hard?" Dango asked.
"A bit," said Suke-san. "Less than yesterday."

Dango sat down beside him.

That afternoon, he tested his paw carefully — a few steps, then more, feeling for the place where the pain would return. It didn't. The paw was fine. He stood and looked at Gabu.

"Tomorrow," Gabu said.
"Yes," said Dango. "Tomorrow."

He sat back down by the stone for one last afternoon, watching the beetle and the birds and the shadows moving slowly across the grass, and he didn't feel any urgency about tomorrow at all.

After

The next morning, Dango ran. His paw held perfectly, and the sensation of moving through the garden again — the ground familiar under his feet, the smells coming fast and layered, the whole circuit opening up in front of him — was deeply satisfying in the way that things are satisfying when you've been without them and then have them back.

He ran the whole loop. He checked everything. The corner by the fence was fine. The rabbit hadn't been. The fat bird in the tree had been joined by a smaller bird of the same kind and they were discussing something intensely. Everything was as it should be.

He was about halfway through his second circuit when he stopped — not because of his paw, and not because he was tired, but because he passed a particular spot in the middle of the open grass and something in him said: here.

He sat down.

He listened.

The thrush at the top of the tree, going up at the end.
The bees in the flower beds, working the late morning.
The wind in the grass, with its own rhythm.
All of it had been here every morning. All of it was still here.

He sat for a while. Then he stood up and ran the rest of the circuit, fast and happy, the way he always ran.

That evening, he mentioned to Gabu that he had stopped in the middle of his circuit on purpose — not to rest, but to listen.

Gabu said: "Good."

Dango thought about this on the walk home. The paw had healed. The circuit was back to normal. Everything was the same.

Except that now he knew — not as an idea but as a felt thing, a thing that lived in his body — that the garden was always doing something interesting, whether or not he was moving through it fast enough to notice.

That seemed like a useful thing to know.

The garden was the same.
But now Dango knew how to listen to it. 🌿✨
