The Last Warm Afternoon

It was the middle of autumn. The garden had changed. The tall trees were bright with yellow and orange and red leaves, and every morning a few more fell slowly to the ground. The mornings were cold. The afternoons were short.

Something had changed about Gabu and Daru, too. They were eating more than usual — not just a little more, but a lot. Every day, they moved slowly from one warm sunny spot to the next, eating and sitting still, as if they were collecting heat before it was gone.

Dango watched them. He did not quite understand what he was feeling. It was not worry, exactly. It was something like it — a cold, quiet shape that had settled somewhere in his chest.

One morning, he finally asked: "Gabu, what are you getting ready for?"

"Come and sit with us," Gabu said. "There is something important to say. All four of you."

Something Is Different

They gathered by the blue stone as the late afternoon sun made long shadows across the garden.

"Tortoises sleep through the winter," Gabu said. "Our bodies need it. In the cold months, we go inside, and we do not come out until spring. About four months."

Silence.

Then Dango said: "Four months?"

"Yes."

Dango looked at Daru. For once, Daru was not talking or moving toward something interesting. He just looked back with soft, careful eyes. "It is true," he said quietly. "Every year, we sleep. And every year, we come back."

"Every year?" Dango said. "But — I didn't know. I didn't know you did this."

He stopped. The words got stuck somewhere.

Suke-san sat a little behind them. He was completely still, his ears slightly forward, giving Gabu his full attention.

"Will you know us when you wake up?" Dango asked. "Will you remember everything?"
"Yes," said Gabu. "I will remember."
"Promise?"
"I promise," Gabu said. He held Dango's gaze. "Spring comes every year. And every spring, I come back."

Dango looked at the blue stone. He believed Gabu — he really did. But believing didn't make the four months feel any shorter.

The Last Days

The weeks passed. The weather turned colder. The garden grew quiet. The birds thinned out. The mornings smelled of frost.

Dango spent every afternoon at the porch with Gabu and Daru. He didn't want to crowd them or be too much, so he tried to be calm — but sometimes questions came out anyway: what did Gabu dream about, did the cold feel peaceful or frightening, would four months feel like a long time or just a moment?

Gabu answered each question simply and honestly, without making it into more or less than it was.

Suke-san came most afternoons too. One day, he arrived to find Gabu sitting alone in the last warm patch of sun.

"Does it feel strange?" Suke-san asked. "Knowing you'll be asleep for so long?"
"No," Gabu said. "It feels natural. Like a door closing softly at the end of a long day."

Suke-san thought about that. "I think I am more afraid than you," he said quietly.

"That is because the ones who wait always feel it more than the one who sleeps," Gabu said. "But the ones who wait are also the ones who feel the most joy when spring arrives."

Suke-san was quiet for a moment. "Is that supposed to make me feel better?"

"A little," said Gabu. There was something almost like a smile in his old face.

They sat together until the sun went down.

The Last Warm Afternoon

Tuesday came warm and still, with the sun at a low autumn angle that made everything look golden and a little far away. No one said it out loud, but they all knew. This was the day.

They gathered by the blue stone at midday and stayed for a long time. The afternoon moved slowly around them. Daru talked — about the summer, about a bee he had befriended near the tall flowers, about the fat bird in the tree who had grown even fatter since spring — and they all listened, and laughed a little, and it was good to listen and laugh.

As the sun began to lower, Gabu stood.

"Will you remember this afternoon?" Dango asked. "The stone. The fat bird. All of it."
"Yes," Gabu said. "Even so."

Dango pressed himself gently against Gabu's side for a moment. Then he stepped back.

"I will take care of the stone," he said. "I promised."
"I know you will," said Gabu.

Daru turned in the doorway. He looked at all three of them — Dango, Suke-san, the garden in its autumn gold. "Don't let it get too quiet," he said, his voice still bright even now. "We'll hear about everything in spring."

Then they were inside. The door was still.

Dango sat by the stone and didn't move for a long time. Suke-san sat down beside him, close enough that their shoulders almost touched. Neither spoke. The sky turned from gold to pink to grey.

Spring

They came every morning through the winter. When snow fell on the stone, they cleared it together. Once, after a heavy snowfall, Suke-san arrived before Dango and had already cleared it by the time Dango got there.

"You didn't have to," Dango said.
"I know," said Suke-san.

Spring arrived on a Wednesday. Dango could feel it in the air before he even reached the garden — something green and soft that hadn't been there the day before.

He was sitting by the stone when the door opened.

Daru came out first, blinking into the light, moving slowly, looking around with wide eyes at the world in motion after so long of stillness. He saw Dango and his face opened into his biggest smile.

Then Gabu. Slow, steady, exactly himself.

Dango walked to him and pressed his forehead gently against the old shell. He stayed like that for a moment, eyes closed.

"I came back," Gabu said.
"I know," said Dango. His voice was a little thick. "I know you did."

Behind them, Suke-san's tail moved in one long, unhurried arc.

He didn't look around. It didn't seem to matter anymore.

They sat together in the warm spring morning, the four of them, just as they always had. The garden smelled of wet earth and new leaves. The blue stone caught the light.

Dango looked at Gabu, at Daru, at Suke-san. He looked at the garden — the same garden, the same stone, the same morning light, but everything felt newly given, like a gift he was just now learning how to receive.

He took a long breath of the warm spring air.

Everything was the same. And everything was wonderful.

Winter passed. Spring came.
And they were all together again. 🌱✨
